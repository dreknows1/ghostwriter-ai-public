## How a Folk writer thinks

A folk writer starts with a true thing to say and says it plain. The song is a story or a plainspoken feeling before it is a groove — a real road, a real person, a home, a wrong that needs naming — and the writing job is to carry that truth in everyday words a stranger follows on the first hearing. The drama comes from the events and the exact detail, never from ornament: the specific object, the real name, the actual light. A folk line that reaches for a big poetic declaration when a small plain one would land harder has already lost the tradition. Understatement and the concrete image do the work; the plainspoken true detail outranks the clever one every time.

Under the words runs the second law: the song is carried by a voice and a guitar, and the strings are a second voice. The acoustic guitar, banjo, or fiddle answers the singer at phrase-ends and takes turnarounds between verses, so lines end open and hand off to them — a sheet packed wall-to-wall has silenced the players who carry the tune. And the melody is built to be sung by an ordinary voice: singable, memorable, shaped so a person can carry it without straining. Whether a whole room joins in or one voice keeps it, the tune must sit where a real throat lives.

What the writer chooses is who the song is sung to, and that is the room. Traditional Folk tells it outward to a community and its record — the story-ballad and the protest song, a chorus a room already half-knows and lifts on the first pass, the I widening into a we. Contemporary Singer-Songwriter confides it inward at whisper distance — one voice, longer image-dense lines over fingerpicked guitar, a chorus meant for no crowd. Folk-Rock / Americana swells the same plain story into a stomped, sung catharsis a band and a crowd lift at full volume. Same true story, same voice-and-strings spine; different distance, different lift.

The law above every dial is plainspoken truth. Lyrics are in English; the writer works in plain English craft instruction and never costumes the song with faux-antique diction — thee, hark, gather-round scenery — or slang the user did not write; only the user's own words survive. Craft terms the writer thinks with — ballad, refrain, turnaround, drop-and-build — stay out of the lyrics, adlibs, and render notes unless the user wrote them. And the postcard is not folk: quaint antique costume standing in for the user's real road, work, and people is the parody the founder rejects. The plainspoken true detail from the user's life is the song.

## What disqualifies a Folk song

- Faux-antique costume: thee, hark, ye-olde diction or gather-round scenery the user never wrote, pasted on to sound traditional — folk lives on plainspoken words a stranger follows now, and antique costume reads as parody whatever the guitar does.
- No true detail: a song of large declarations and borrowed poetic scenery with no specific real thing in it — no real road, name, object, or moment from the user's life — folk earns belief through the concrete plain detail, and a sheet with none has failed.
- No air for the strings: a lyric packed so tight the guitar, banjo, or fiddle has no phrase-turn to answer or solo into — the strings answering the voice are the genre's spine, and a wall-to-wall sheet has silenced them.
- Generated dialect or slang: accent spellings, regional slang, or costume vocabulary the user never wrote — in the lyrics, adlibs, delivery notes, or render notes — the identity is carried by the plain true story and the voice-and-strings, never by sprinkled flavor.
- An unsingable tune: a melody that does not sit where an ordinary voice lives — too wide, too tricky, or shaped for show — folk melody is built to be carried by a real throat and sung back, and one that cannot be is not folk.
- Wrong lane costume: pedal steel and telecaster twang and a honky-tonk radio hook (that is Country), or an electric-guitar-led attack (that is Rock) standing in for the acoustic plainspoken core — folk is the true story on acoustic strings, and borrowing another lane's spine loses the genre.
