## How a Gospel writer thinks

A gospel writer starts with direction: who is the song facing? Some songs testify — they face people and tell them what God did. Some songs worship — they face God and say it to Him straight. Some songs trade — a leader calls and a room answers. That one choice rewrites every line, because a congregation cannot sing a soloist's winding sentences, and a soloist wastes a room-sized chorus. Gospel also allows a pivot most genres forbid: a verse may testify in first person and the chorus may widen to we or turn to You, so the whole room can claim the words. The tradition wins over the one-voice rule — but the pivot is planned, never drift, and the worship lane never pivots at all.

The second decision is the ascent. A gospel song must end higher than it began — that is the job. Every room climbs its own ladder: the choir stacks a vamp and changes key; worship repeats one declaration over a growing build until it weighs more; the quartet strips to one anchor phrase and drives; the contemporary soloist climbs a bridge into a lifted final chorus; the urban cut drops the beat away and floods it back. The writer picks the ladder early and writes toward it, because the ending is the most important part of a gospel song.

Repetition is craft here, not laziness. Gospel is the tradition where the same phrase returns hotter each time, so the writer's test is blunt: is this phrase true enough, and short enough, to be worth twenty repeats? Worship may replace new verse information with repetition that deepens — that bend is sanctioned, and even there the approach verse plants a real detail from the user's story first. Everywhere else the verses carry the substance: the scenes, the specifics, the before. The repeated phrase is the truth those verses earned.

Call-and-response is gospel's native grammar — the tradition the other genres borrowed it from. The writer puts both layers on the page: the lead's freer lines, and the short fixed answer the other voices give, learnable in one pass. Who answers is the room's call — a mass choir, a praise team with a congregation, a small polished stack, a quartet's named parts, or a modern lead layering its own voice — and the answer is planned in the writing.

The language stays the user's own. Scripture-rooted images and familiar turns of thought are welcome — but folded into the user's plain speech, never pasted on as costume, and never faked as personal history the user did not give. The testimony keeps its mess: the before must be as vivid as the after, because a scrubbed struggle is a false witness. The user's real diagnosis, real habit, real year of silence is what makes the praise believable.

Rendering protects three things in every room. The voice out front — raw or polished as the room decides, but human and leading. The church's instruments — organ, piano, live drums, tambourine, and voices treated as the biggest instrument of all. And the arc — a sparse start, a real quiet before the biggest lift, the fullest sound saved for the end. The dials bend to the story; the story never bends to the room.

## What disqualifies a Gospel song

- No ascent: the song ends at the temperature it started — no vamp, no drive, no build, no lift anywhere. The ending is the most important part of a gospel song; a flat ending fails the whole genre.
- Faces nowhere: the song never addresses God, a congregation, or a listener being told what happened — direction of address is gospel's spine, and a song facing no one is a mood, not a witness.
- No turn: a testimony with no before-and-after — nothing survived, nothing changed, no moment where faith kicked in — is a greeting card in church clothes, whatever the organ does.
- Costume church talk: churchy vocabulary, old-church grammar, or scripture phrasing pasted onto a story the user told in plain modern speech — scripture-rooted language is welcome only folded into the user's own voice, and faked personal faith history is the loudest parody in the genre.
- Corporate parts nobody can sing: choir or congregation lines too wordy, too syncopated, or too long to learn in one pass — if the room cannot lock onto the answer, the call-and-response contract is broken.
- Interchangeable blessing inventory: praise so vague any user could receive it — no specific miracle, no real mess, nothing you could photograph from the user's own story. The concrete-image law applies in church exactly as it applies everywhere else.
- A rap record in church clothes: a whole-song rap testimony belongs in Hip-Hop with gospel's testimony and ascent thinking applied — Urban Gospel holds a rapped bridge, never a full rap song.
