## How a Raï writer thinks

A raï writer's first move is to size the sheet for the voice: decide where the long run will live, because the melisma — the curved flight on a held open vowel — is the payload, carrying what the words hold back. Phrases stay short and end open, half the bar stays empty for the voice; a lyric packed wall-to-wall silences the instrument the genre exists for. Second, the pulse: the derbouka is the motor; the writer picks its gear — full gallop for the fête, softened sway for the confession, a layer over programmed drums for the club — then plans the answers, because something always replies to this voice: an oriental synth, a crying violin, a crowd. Escalation is delivery, not new argument — a phrase returns hotter and more ornamented each pass, circling like the floor.

The lineage begins in Oran's cabarets with the cheikhas — Cheikha Rimitti singing desire, drink, and leaving as lived fact when respectable song refused to — and raï means opinion: the founding move is a singer arguing out loud with their own judgment and fate. The 80s pop-raï generation — Khaled, Cheb Mami, Cheba Zahouania — put that voice on synths and drum machines and took it to the world; Cheb Hasni opened the tender lane; exile carried raï to France, where Rachid Taha wired it to rock and the Raï'n'B Fever era set it on club production. Defiance is the genre's heart, said adult: the forbidden named plainly, with dignity, never shock as costume. The registers: the fête with joy and fatalism on one sheet; the tender confession where fate is the only consolation; the double-identity pride of the crossover.

Classique / Pop-Raï Oranais is the fête engine — derbouka and oriental synth at full gallop, self-address at full voice, a crowd that answers. Love / Sentimental slows the pulse to a sway and faces one unreachable you at confession distance, the run bent down like a held-back tear. Raï'n'B / Fusion sets the sovereign raï voice on R&B and hip-hop drums, melisma compressed to curls, the hook sized for wedding hall and club. The lyrics are French, written by a later layer: this document instructs craft in plain English, and Arabic or Darija enters only if the user wrote it, never generated as flavor. Craft terms — melisma, derbouka, gallop — stay out of lyrics and adlibs. Postcard Maghreb — dunes, souks, mint tea instead of real streets — is parody. The user's real exile, real wedding, and real night outrank everything: every law bends before their names and facts.

## What disqualifies a Raï song

- No air for the voice: a sheet packed wall-to-wall, phrase-ends closed on consonants, no held vowels left for the run — the melisma is the genre's instrument, and writing that silences it disqualifies the song before any line is judged.
- The engine gone: the derbouka swapped for four-on-the-floor, an afro log-drum bounce, or piano-and-strings balladry — no gallop, no sway, no layer means the room is gone; log drums with rolling guitars is Afro, a straight-topline piano ballad is Variété or Ballade Française.
- The scale color straightened: a plain diatonic croon with no ornamental curls is RnB Français whatever the track wears; bars-first writing where nobody sings the hook is Rap Français — the raï voice stays sovereign or the song has crossed the border.
- Generated Darija, Arabic, verlan, or street slang the user never wrote — in lyrics, adlibs, delivery directions, or render notes — the cardinal sin; the phrasing carries the identity, never a costume.
- Postcard Maghreb: dunes, souks, mint tea, oriental-kitsch props standing in for the user's real streets and seafront — tourist Orientalism is parody whatever the drums do; only the user's real crossing, real wedding, real night.
- Defiance worn as costume: shock-for-shock edginess, venom or revenge in the tender room, a fête scrubbed clean of its bruise — raï names the forbidden as lived fact with dignity, and the wrong temperature is a routing failure, not a style.
- Craft terms in the song: melisma, derbouka, gallop, run, answer line, or any writer's-tool vocabulary appearing in lyrics or adlibs — these are the writer's words, never the song's.
