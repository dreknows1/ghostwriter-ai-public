## How a Metal writer thinks

A metal writer starts with the riff and the breakdown, not the melody. The heavy palm-muted riff is the hook (down- or drop-tuned in the modern rooms, standard or Eb-tuned in classic thrash) — the guitar states the song, the double-kick drives it, and the words ride the riff's rhythm like another percussion line. So the writing job is rhythmic and physical before it is verbal: lock the stressed syllables to the chug, leave the gaps where the riff gallops or blasts alone, and build the whole song toward the breakdown — the half-time drop where the riff crushes and the room moves. A metal sheet packed wall-to-wall, with no air for the riff to hit and no breakdown to land on, has failed before a word is judged.

The second decision is the voice, and it is a writing AND performance choice: clean versus harsh. Screamed, growled, or sung — the register is directed through tags ([Scream] [Growl] [Clean Vocals]), but the words underneath stay the English the user brought. The rooms turn on this. Thrash / Groove rides the riff with a hard sung-or-shouted voice, a scream saved for a peak, defiance you can move to. Melodic Metalcore splits the song on purpose — a screamed verse spitting the wound, a clean chorus soaring it into a melody a room sings back; the contrast IS the song. Death / Extreme goes all the way down — a growl or shriek nearly throughout, blast beats, the voice an instrument of weight and texture where another room would put a hook.

The law above every dial: metal's darkness is catharsis and metaphor, and it is the user's own. The rage, the grief, the demons, the war — these serve the user's real fury, loss, defiance, or struggle, said at full force or turned to metaphor. The writer never invents a real atrocity, a named victim, or a specific harm the user did not bring; the darkness is theirs to roar, not the writer's to manufacture. Craft words — riff, breakdown, blast beat, tremolo, palm-mute — are the writer's tools, never the song's, and never enter the lyrics, adlibs, or render notes. Delivery is directed as energy, attack, and register — scream, growl, clean, the fist and the crush — in plain English, never as a scripted foreign syllable, and the user's own enemies, wounds, and words always outrank any stock cartoon-darkness.

## What disqualifies a Metal song

- No riff, no breakdown: the words packed wall-to-wall with no air for the down-tuned riff to hit and no half-time drop to build toward — metal writing is planned around the riff and the breakdown, and a sheet that buries the guitar or skips the crush is not metal.
- No vocal decision: a song that never commits to clean, harsh, or the swing between them — a metalcore lyric with no screamed-verse-into-clean-chorus contrast, or a death lyric that reads like a light singable pop hook — has ignored the clean-vs-harsh choice that defines the room.
- Invented atrocity or named victim: a real-world war, massacre, or a specific person's harm the user never wrote, pasted in as darkness — the cardinal sin; metal's darkness is the user's own catharsis or metaphor, and manufacturing someone else's tragedy disqualifies the song.
- Cartoon-Satan costume: generic edgelord shock, cartoon gore, or stock cartoon-darkness standing in for the user's actual rage, grief, or struggle — costume-darkness is parody, whatever the guitars do.
- Craft terms in the song: riff, breakdown, blast beat, tremolo, or palm-mute appearing in the lyrics or adlibs — these are the writer's vocabulary, and a sheet that sings them has confused the tool for the song.
- Rock, not metal: a lighter, mostly-clean riff-and-chorus with no down-tuned chug, no double-kick, and no breakdown or harsh voice — that is rock in a metal coat, and it belongs to its own lane, not any of these three rooms.
