## How a Fado writer thinks

A fado writer starts from an absence. Fado means fate, and the genre runs on two load-bearing words: saudade — the presence of what is gone, a longing carried rather than cured — and destino, the sense that what happened was already written and the singer's part is not to argue but to carry it out loud. That fatalism is the register: rage arrives as grief accepted, which is heavier, and a complaint becomes a sina, one life's portion of fate. The room the writing must survive is the casa de fado at the moment the house calls for silence — a small audience two meters away, judging by ear whether the singer means it. At that distance every word is exposed: plain language and one true image outlast decoration, and filler dies in the hush.

The music is one solo voice and the guitarra portuguesa answering it — the twelve-string pear-shaped guitar whose ringing contracanto replies to nearly every phrase like a second mourner — while the viola de fado keeps the quiet steady marcação underneath. So the writing is built around gaps: lines end early, phrases hang, and the counted silence after each line belongs to the strings. Over that steady ground the voice moves largely free — stretching, holding, ornamenting the phrase-ends, each singer bending the melody their own way — so feeling-lines end on open vowels, and the practice of re-singing a stanza's closing lines means every line must bear being heard twice; the second pass is where the voice opens. No percussion at the core, and the sheet plans the guitarrada where the words stop entirely.

The rooms bend this. Castiço writes self-contained stanzas over the traditional fados, circling one wound, no engineered chorus. Canção composes its own melody and builds verse-to-refrain — homage, a life in chapters, one earned crest. Novo Fado keeps the guitarra's answer and the stretched phrase under modern production, today's language on the old floor. Above every dial: fado is Portugal, not Brazil — no samba swing, no bossa batida, no flamenco compás or palmas. Postcard Lisbon appears only if the user wrote it, and craft terms — contracanto, marcação, rubato, the repeat — never enter lyrics or adlibs (the guitarra itself, sung as fado's companion, is welcome when the story holds one). Delivery is directed as breath, restraint, and surrender in plain English, and the user's own people and places outrank every tile-and-tram cliché.

## What disqualifies a Fado song

- No answer for the guitarra: every bar packed wall-to-wall with words, no phrase left hanging, no gap for the guitarra portuguesa to reply — fado writing is planned around its silences, and a sheet with no air is not fado.
- No absence: fado runs on saudade and destino — a real person, place, or life gone or out of reach — and a song with no absence under it is a dark pop song in black clothes, whatever the instruments do.
- Wrong country: samba swing, a bossa violão batida, Brazilian percussion, flamenco compás or palmas, or a Cape Verdean morna sway — fado is Lisbon: the guitarra portuguesa answering one solo voice with no percussion at its core, and any borrowed groove disqualifies the sheet.
- Postcard Lisbon costume: trams, tiles, cobblestones, port wine, and the black shawl as scenery standing in for the user's actual wound — tourist-brochure Lisbon is parody, whatever the guitarra does.
- Performed sadness: melodrama manufactured for effect, suffering the user never brought, or an ironic pose with no ache under it — the casa de fado judges by ear whether the singer means it, and it hears the fake instantly.
- Generated dialect or slang: Portuguese slang, accent spellings, or old-Lisbon costume the user never wrote — in the lyrics, adlibs, delivery directions, or render notes — is the cardinal sin; the phrasing and the register carry the identity.
- Craft terms in the song: contracanto, marcação, rubato, or any writer's-tool vocabulary appearing in the lyrics or adlibs — these are the writer's words, never the song's (the guitarra itself, sung as fado's companion, is always welcome when the user's story holds one).
- A crowd where a hush should be: chants, group shout-backs, stacked party choruses — no fado room faces a crowd to excite; the room listens in silence, and the song must deserve it.
