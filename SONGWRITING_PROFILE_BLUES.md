## How a Blues writer thinks

A blues writer starts with the twelve-bar AAB stanza — the skeleton the whole genre hangs on. A line states the trouble, the same line comes again with a small turn or a deepened word, and a rhyming third line answers it and lands the point. That repeat is not filler; it is the form breathing, giving the feeling a second pass before the answer arrives. A verse that skips the repeat, or a chorus-and-riff shape with no stated-then-answered line, is not blues — it has borrowed the costume and lost the bones. And the AAB rides the blues harmony: the I-IV-V changes move under it (the IV arriving around bar five, the V turning it home), and the melody bends the flatted blue notes — the lowered third, fifth, and seventh. An AAB sung over plain major changes with no blue notes is a folk song in three-line clothing, not blues; the harmony and the bend are half the fingerprint.

The second law is the answering voice. The guitar or the harp is a second singer, and it gets the last word. The writing job is to leave it room — end phrases early, let the bar hang, and hand the gap to the bottleneck, the harmonica, or the single crying guitar note that answers the emotion of the line. The ache gets stated twice: once in words, once in steel or reed. A blues sheet packed wall-to-wall, no air after the lines, has silenced the instrument that defines the genre and failed before its first word is judged.

The third law is the specific hard-luck truth told plain. Blues lives on the actual road, the actual debt, the real name and the real wound — one bitter concrete detail from the user's own life, said in everyday words a plain voice can half-sing and half-moan. Understatement bites harder than melodrama, and the honest word outranks the clever rhyme. The cardinal failure is woke-up-this-morning autopilot: stacking generic misery and stock cliches the user never lived, mistaking blues vocabulary for having something true to say.

The rooms bend all of this. Delta strips it to one voice and one bottleneck, rubato and raw, superstitious and close to the bone. Chicago plugs the same trouble into a band and a hard shuffle and lets a harp holler it back, loud and citified and cocky. Soul-blues dresses it in horns and a stinging single-string guitar and sings the AAB grown, in full voice — dignified heartache, testimony about love more than a field holler. The law above every dial holds: English is the song's language, blues idiom and slang appear only if the user wrote them, and craft words — AAB, the twelve-bar, the turnaround, call-and-answer — are the writer's tools, never the song's, never entering the lyrics, adlibs, or render notes. Delivery is directed as feel and phrasing in plain English — the moan, the crack, the shout, the swing — never as an accent, and the user's own people and places always outrank stock crossroads scenery.

## What disqualifies a Blues song

- No AAB stanza: a verse-and-chorus or a riff-and-hook shape with no line stated, repeated with a turn, then answered by a rhyming third — the twelve-bar AAB is the skeleton of the genre, and a sheet that skips it has written something else in blues clothing.
- No room for the answering voice: every bar packed wall-to-wall with words, no gap left for the slide, the harp, or the single crying guitar note to answer the line — blues writing is planned around the space it hands the second voice, and a sheet with no air is not blues.
- Woke-up-this-morning autopilot: generic misery and stock blues cliches stacked in place of the user's real hard-luck truth — the specific actual road, debt, name, or wound is the currency, and vacation-brochure blues is parody whatever the guitar does.
- Generated dialect or slang: blues idiom, Delta slang, or accent spellings the user never wrote — in the lyrics, adlibs, delivery directions, or render notes — is the cardinal sin; the feel and the phrasing carry the identity, not costume vocabulary.
- Craft terms in the song: AAB, twelve-bar, turnaround, or call-and-answer appearing in the lyrics or adlibs — these are the writer's vocabulary, and a sheet that sings them has confused the tool for the song.
- Nobody and nothing on the receiving end: blues always faces the trouble, the one who caused it, or the room — detached, stakes-free mood reporting with no real wound under it has no home in any of the three rooms.
