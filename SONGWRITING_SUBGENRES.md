# Sub-Genres — Same Genre, Different Rooms (DRAFT for founder review)

> Companion to SONGWRITING_BRAIN.md. Same laws apply everywhere in this document:
> no example lyric lines, no generated dialect, conventions are dials never templates,
> and the user's story always outranks the room it's told in.
> Status: DRAFT — nothing built from this until the founder approves it.

---

## Part 1 — What a sub-genre is

A sub-genre is the same genre in a different room. A bachata love song and a reggaeton love song are both Latin, but the rooms are different: bachata feels like a slow dance — not because the drums are slow (on the clock they often aren't), but because the words are sparse and the phrases leave long gaps so the voice can ache and the guitar can answer — while reggaeton packs short punchy phrases tight onto the drum, built for a club. The love story is still 100% the user's; the sub-genre only decides the room it's told in — how long the sentences breathe, how many words sit on the beat, and where the emotion is allowed to get loud. That's why 'a bachata love song' comes out different from 'a Latin love song': naming the room pre-loads all those settings before the first word is written.

## Part 2 — How the brain lands on ONE room per song

- RULE 1 — An explicit user pick always wins. If the user names a sub-genre in the picker or inside their own story ('make it drill', 'a bachata love song'), that is the sub-genre, period. Era requests count as picks too: '90s R&B' or '70s soul' maps to a named era page if the genre has one; if not, use the parent page and carry the era as a writing-flavor note — never silently drop the decade into the default. If we don't yet have a page for the pick, use the nearest parent page we DO have and stamp the song record 'not-yet-deep' so the founder can see it — never pretend depth we haven't built.
- RULE 2 — Otherwise, infer from strong story cues only. Cue-matching runs only inside the genre the user already chose — the genre pick itself comes from the app's input, and sub-genre landing never crosses genres. The brief-builder reads the story's purpose, setting, and pace words (slow dance, church vamp, block party, windows down) and matches them against cue lists written on each sub-genre's curriculum page. Every cue on a page is marked strong (fires alone) or weak (needs a partner): landing takes one strong cue, or two weak cues pointing the same way. The 'Landing here' sections in this document are the founder-readable source for those lists: when the curriculum is compiled, each cue in that prose becomes a per-page list entry marked strong or weak, and the founder approves the marks. The machine only counts marks — it never judges — and cue lists live in the curriculum, where the founder edits them like everything else.
- RULE 3 — When cues are weak or split, land on the genre's declared safe default. Every genre's Composer Profile names one mainstream default sub-genre (the broad center — the one a stranger would picture). One correction the brain needs: 'Reggae / Afrobeats' is listed as one priority slot, but they are two different genres — each gets its own Composer Profile and its own declared default before this rule can touch them; the combined listing is a priority grouping, not one genre. The machine may NEVER guess its way to a rare or specialist sub-genre — those are reached only by Rule 1 or a strong Rule 2 cue.
- RULE 4 — Exactly one sub-genre per song, even for fusions. If the user explicitly asks for a blend ('trap soul', 'gospel trap', 'afro-fusion'), check for a page under that fusion name first — some blends earn their own page. If none exists, the sub-genre that sets tempo and groove takes the spec slot, the other survives only as a writing-flavor note, and the record is stamped 'not-yet-deep'. The same tie-break applies when an inferred story straddles two rooms. Two half-specs make a mush song.
- RULE 5 — The story outranks the sub-genre, always. If the chosen sub-genre's conventions fight the story (a grief story inferred into a party sub-genre), first re-check the inference; if the user picked it explicitly, keep the pick but bend the conventions to fit the story — never trim, brighten, or reshape the story to fit the conventions. Conventions are dials, never templates.
- RULE 6 — The decision is recorded, shown to the user, and gate-graded. The brief logs which rule fired (picked / inferred / defaulted) and what cues triggered it, and the chosen sub-genre is SHOWN to the user — especially on defaults and 'not-yet-deep' landings, never a silent swap: a user who asked for bachata must see what room they actually got. Wrong results get one plain reason tag from the gate's single tag list (hook, flow, generic, story-mismatch, genre-false, wrong room, wrong writing in the right room, other) in the founder's 20-song gate; the two entries on that list that belong to sub-genres are 'wrong room' (the landing failed — fix the cue list) and 'wrong writing in the right room' (the page failed — fix its dials).
- RULE 7 — No sub-genre may ever introduce dialect the user didn't write — in the lyrics OR in the render vocabulary — quoted example lines, or a house formula. These laws sit above every rule here; a sub-genre page that needs them to sound authentic is a failed page.

## Part 3 — What the chosen room changes (and what it can never touch)

Once one sub-genre is chosen, its curriculum page fills in the Step-1 brief's musical spec DEFAULTS: tempo range, groove feel (straight, swung, or half-time — where the drums feel half as fast), bars per section, and how densely words sit on that tempo (when a page leaves bars-per-section unset, that default falls to the genre profile or the per-song brief). It also swings the writing dials the drafts are judged against: phrasing length, rhyme density (heavy internal rhyme vs. barely any), which creative devices are at home here (double meanings, scripture echoes, flips), vocal space (how much room for runs and ad-libs — the small extra vocal sounds between lines), section conventions (does this room vamp — repeat a short musical phrase over and over — does it crown the chorus, does it flip the flow mid-song), and point-of-view norms where the tradition bends the one-voice rule. It also hands the render step its native vocabulary: the Suno-facing tags, ad-lib notes, and vamp habits that make the renderer play this room and not a generic one — and that vocabulary obeys the dialect law too: ad-libs are sounds and delivery notes (hums, calls, echoes, breath), never slang words or dialect spellings, unless the user's own story supplied them. Language stays the user's pick: a sub-genre page never sets or switches the song's language — a bachata page must write true bachata phrasing in whatever language the user chose (subject to the plan's English-only buildout for now). Everything the page sets is a DEFAULT — the per-song brief may still override any dial when this particular story demands it, and the same code checks (per-song math, metric parallels — repeated lines with matching syllable counts and stresses — and singable endings) verify the result. What a sub-genre may NEVER override: the user's story — their specifics, core emotion, and the turn are untouchable, because sub-genre decides HOW the writing thinks, never WHAT the song is about — and every law in Rule 7 (originality, dialect, no example lines), which governs here exactly as written there. Same test as genres: if two sub-genre pages would produce the same lyric sheet, one of them isn't a real sub-genre page yet.

## Part 4 — Any genre, later, no new engineering

This machinery is one mechanism that reads pages — it never hard-codes any sub-genre. Each genre's Composer Profile grows a short sub-genre section: a named list, one curriculum page per sub-genre, and every page holds the same fixed slots (musical-spec defaults, writing dials, render vocabulary of sounds-not-slang, a story-cue list with every cue marked strong or weak (the 'Landing here' prose in this document is the founder-readable source — compiling the curriculum turns each cue into a marked list entry, and the founder approves the marks), era mappings like '90s R&B' pointing to a named page (in practice these mappings live in the genre's landing notes and the page names themselves), and what disqualifies a song in that room), plus the profile's one declared safe default. So when Latin arrives later and the founder wants bachata: nobody touches code. Someone writes one bachata page to the same standard — plain language, no example lines, real writing differences, passes the two-pages-same-sheet test and the build's no-quoted-lines check, fits the size budget (the pages in this document are teaching text; each compiles down to a compact writer card of roughly 600 tokens of dials and spec defaults, and the compiled per-song slice — the universal core, the genre profile, and the ONE sub-genre card — is capped at roughly 2,000 tokens) — the founder approves it, and Rules 1 through 7 immediately know how to land on it, fill the brief from it, show it to the user, and grade it through the same 20-song gate. A genre with zero sub-genre pages just runs on its parent profile with Rule 3's default, clearly labeled 'not-yet-deep'. New rooms are new curriculum, never new engineering.

# The five priority genres


## R&B

**Why the room matters here:** R&B sub-genres mostly share the same subjects — love, desire, honesty, loss — so the subject can never tell you how to write. What separates them is how many words fit in a line, how neat the rhymes are, how guarded or open the heart sounds, and where the singer gets room to run. Write a guarded late-night story with big 90s group-chorus rules and the song cheers when it should brood; write an anniversary song with clipped trap-soul phrasing and the devotion reads as coldness. Because the topics overlap so heavily, the writing dials are the only thing keeping an R&B song from collapsing into one generic love song.

**What really separates these rooms:** Axis 1 — Word density and phrase shape: from Quiet Storm's few slow blooming words, to Contemporary's conversational spill that runs past the bar like speech, to 2000s R&B's full narrative sentences that resolve tidily at line ends, to Trap-Soul's clipped 3-to-7-word phrases surrounded by silence, to Classic Soul's punchy on-the-beat symmetry. Axis 2 — Emotional guard and drama: Classic Soul and 90s R&B beg and swear at full sincerity, 2000s R&B turns feeling into events and confrontation, Contemporary confesses messily, Neo-Soul meditates, Quiet Storm cherishes, Trap-Soul admits things flatly with the guard still up. Axis 3 — Where the vocal athleticism lives: runs invited at every line-end (90s), one solo star showing off at the peaks (2000s), saved for one final vamp — the chorus looping while the lead sings freely over it (Quiet Storm, Classic Soul), replaced entirely by tone and repetition (Trap-Soul), or loosened into elastic behind-the-beat drift (Neo-Soul) — and the lyric has to be written to leave that specific kind of room.


### Contemporary R&B
*Today's mainstream R&B — diary-honest, melodic, streaming-era songs that feel like a late-night text thread set to music.*

- **Tempo & groove:** 65–105 BPM. Ballads sit 65–80 with half-time drums; mid-tempos ride 90–105 with a light bounce. Word density is moderate and conversational — phrases the length of spoken sentences with natural pauses, never packed bars.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Phrasing runs conversational and uneven on purpose — a line can spill past the bar like real speech, then the next one stops short; too-perfect symmetry reads as fake in this style.
  - Rhyme stays loose: slant rhyme and vowel echoes over perfect rhyme; a perfectly rhymed four-line block sounds like a greeting card here, where it would sound right in Classic Soul.
  - Vulnerability is the default register — flaws, jealousy, and mixed feelings get said plainly; sensuality shows up as honesty about wanting someone, not as seduction talk.
  - Write what the user would text: specific, present-tense, small details pulled from their actual story (the place, the time of night, the exact habit) beat poetic images every time.
  - Leave ad-lib space at line ends and behind the hook — keep the main line short enough that an answer vocal can echo or comment underneath it.
  - The double entendre lives in the hook and plays it casual — one phrase that reads as love and self-worth at once, dropped without being announced.
  - POV: present tense — the song sounds like it is being said tonight, not remembered from years away.
  - Choruses lift melodically but stay low-key — a raised refrain, not a belted anthem; the bridge often drops to near-spoken honesty, the most naked moment of the song.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Warm sparse keys or a clean electric guitar loop over sub-heavy 808 bass, with soft half-time snares and plenty of air between elements. Lead vocal close-miked and intimate, stacked harmonies arriving on the hook, answer ad-libs tucked behind the lead. Modern polish with light vocal effects — a 2010s-to-now sheen, never busy.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for real, messy, current relationship stories — situationships, self-worth after a breakup, healing, wanting someone you shouldn't, complicated love told honestly. Serves badly: formal tributes, whole-life story songs, weddings that want grandeur, or anything needing big-band ceremony.
- **What makes it a parody:** Over-singing every line instead of talking some of them; pet-name filler and stock compliments instead of the user's actual details; tidy nursery rhymes; announcing the sexy or sad part instead of just saying the true thing; filling the space that should stay empty for ad-libs.

### 90s R&B
*The golden-era sound — big stacked harmonies, hip-hop drums under grown love songs, and choruses built to be belted by a group.*

- **Tempo & groove:** Ballads 60–76 BPM with heavy swung sixteenth-notes (the fast notes lean long-short instead of even); hip-hop-soul mid-tempos high-80s to mid-100s over a boom-bap backbeat (the snare cracking on beats 2 and 4 — classic head-nod hip-hop drums); new-jack-swing uptempos (the late-80s style that put hip-hop drums under R&B songs) roughly 104–116 with a hard bounce. Word density low-to-moderate in verses; choruses are short and endlessly repeatable.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - The chorus is an anthem: short, declarative, metrically parallel lines (matching syllable counts and stresses) built for group harmony — this is the one sibling where the chorus must survive four voices belting it at once.
  - Verses are pleading and direct — full sentences of devotion, apology, or desire aimed straight at the person; less irony and more open commitment than any other R&B sub-genre.
  - Run-room is a writing job: end key lines on open vowels and leave the last beat or two of the bar empty so the lead can run; a syllable-packed line ending kills the style. This is the firewall against Classic Soul: 90s verses are full conversational sentences that stop early to leave room for runs, where Classic Soul verses are short stress-locked lines punched right on the beat.
  - Rhyme lands tidier than modern R&B — end rhymes hit more reliably — but the honest word still outranks the rhyming one.
  - Sensuality is confident and stated (grown and unashamed) while vulnerability's natural home is the bridge — the begging, the confession, the admission of fault usually lands there; but if the user's story IS the confession, the story wins and the whole song can carry it.
  - Call-and-response is structural, not decoration: write lead lines that a background stack can answer, and plan a repeated end-vamp (the chorus loops while the lead sings freely over it).
  - Double entendres go bolder here — the slow-jam tradition lets one physical image carry the song's second meaning, planted in verse one and paid off in the hook.
  - POV: full sincerity, no detached narration — and the address stays locked on that one person to the last note; it never widens to the room the way Classic Soul does.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Boom-bap or live-feel drums under lush keys (Rhodes warmth, bell-tone synths), real moving bass lines, and big stacked background harmonies treated as their own instrument. Gospel-trained lead vocal upfront with runs at phrase ends, church-flavored chord changes, and an extended outro vamp — a late-80s-through-90s radio warmth throughout (the new-jack-swing option sits at the earlier end of that window).
- **Stories it serves:** Best for devotion, weddings, apologies, winning somebody back, grown committed romance, and friend-group anthems that want harmony and lift. Serves badly: detached or ironic stories, guarded narrators, and minimal lo-fi moods — this style cannot keep its heart hidden.
- **What makes it a parody:** Cramming in dated pet names and 90s slang the user never wrote (the era lives in the chords and harmonies, not the vocabulary); turning every single line into a run cue; choruses that are paragraphs instead of chants; wearing the decade as a costume instead of writing sincere devotion.

### 2000s R&B
*The turn-of-the-millennium sound — glossy production and dramatic story-songs where one star voice narrates the night everything changed.*

- **Tempo & groove:** Ballads 60–80 BPM with big dramatic peaks; mid-tempos 88–105 over crisp programmed drums with a smooth head-nod bounce; club-leaning uptempos up to about 115. Word density moderate-to-high in verses — full narrative sentences that fill the bar — then choruses tighten into short, clean, resolving lines.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Verses tell a story in order — set the scene, then the event, then the fallout; verse two advances the plot like a second act. No sibling narrates like this: Contemporary confesses present-tense feelings, 90s pleads straight at the person.
  - The chorus is one clean, complete statement of the situation, sung by a single star voice — tidier than Contemporary's loose refrain, but never built for the four-voice group belt of 90s R&B.
  - Drama is allowed to peak theatrically — the ultimatum, the discovery, the confession said out loud — where Contemporary would underplay it and Quiet Storm would forbid it.
  - Run-room serves one show-off voice: leave open line-endings at the emotional peaks for a solo run; background vocals echo the lead's words instead of answering them — the opposite of 90s call-and-response.
  - Rhyme resolves at line ends more reliably than Contemporary because the narrative wants closure, but stays looser than Classic Soul's clean perfect rhymes.
  - The bridge is the plot twist: the reveal, the decision, the thing the listener didn't know yet — not just a deeper feeling.
  - POV: often starts in past tense telling what happened, then flips to present at the hook where the feeling lands now — the only sibling where past-tense storytelling is the default.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Glossy programmed drums with crisp snares, plush keys or plucked string-like synth lines, deep polished bass, dramatic string or choir pads at the peaks. One star lead vocal front and center with confident runs and doubled hooks, background vocals echoing the lead rather than answering it — an early-2000s radio shine, glossier than 90s warmth, fuller than trap-soul's empty space.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for stories with a plot: a confession, a confrontation, a dramatic apology, the night everything changed, a breakup with actual events in it. Serves badly: quiet contentment, meditative growth stories, and whisper-close intimacy — this style needs something to happen.
- **What makes it a parody:** Era props (flip phones, club scenes, the other woman) the user never gave; melodrama with no real event underneath it; over-running every line instead of saving runs for the peaks; letting the era's stock plots swallow the user's actual story.

### Neo-Soul
*Loose, live, poetic soul — jazz chords, behind-the-beat grooves, and lyrics that read like spoken poetry about love and self.*

- **Tempo & groove:** 60–95 BPM with a heavily behind-the-beat, slightly drunken swing — the drums drag on purpose and the voice floats over the bar line. Word density is flexible: lines can be talky and dense or stretch one thought across four bars; either way the phrasing never sits squarely on the grid.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Phrasing is elastic: start lines late, let them cross bar lines, resolve where natural speech would resolve — write for a singer who treats the beat as a suggestion, the opposite of Classic Soul's on-the-beat punch.
  - Imagery does the heavy lifting — abstract feelings must become physical pictures (a place, a body, a meal, weather from the user's world) — but every image must be built from the user's own story details, never pulled from the genre's stock shelf; this is the most metaphor-rich of all the siblings.
  - The subject widens past romance: self-knowledge, spirit, community, growth — a neo-soul love song is always also about who the narrator is becoming.
  - Rhyme is optional and mostly internal — vowel sounds inside the lines carry the musicality; forced end-rhyme flattens the poetry and marks the writer as an outsider.
  - Sensuality is slow-burn and adult, carried through metaphor rather than statement; vulnerability sounds like meditation, not confession or begging.
  - Repetition works as mantra: a phrase returns with small changes until it deepens — choruses are allowed to circle rather than lift, unlike every other sibling.
  - Write fewer words than feel necessary and leave long instrumental pockets — the band, the hum, and the silence are co-writers.
  - The double entendre is a spirit/body twin — one image that means both the lover and the higher thing, sustained rather than punchlined.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Fender Rhodes with extended jazz chords, warm live bass, dusty in-the-pocket drums that drag a hair behind, muted guitar licks, optional horns. Vocals layered loose and human — hummed ad-libs, spoken asides, minimal pitch correction — with a late-90s/early-2000s organic, analog warmth and room to breathe.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for reflective stories — personal growth, self-love, gratitude, long-term love seen from altitude, roots and family lineage, spiritual searching. Serves badly: urgent drama, petty conflict, club energy, or any story that needs to move fast and hit hard on the beat.
- **What makes it a parody:** Fake-deep word salad with no concrete story underneath; mystical props stacked up as decoration; imitating vocal scatting or filler syllables on the page; poeticness with zero specific detail from the user's actual life — the poetry must be built FROM their story, not draped over it.

### Trap-Soul / Alt-R&B
*Moody late-night R&B over trap drums — short melodic phrases, blunt honesty, atmosphere over polish.*

- **Tempo & groove:** Felt tempo 60–80 BPM in half-time (often written 120–160 with rolling hi-hats doubling and tripling above the slow snare). Word density is the lowest-per-bar of the modern siblings: short clipped phrases with real dead air between them, and heavy repetition doing melodic work.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Phrases run short and clipped — roughly three to seven words, dropped like texts, each followed by space; long flowing sentences break the style instantly.
  - Repetition IS the hook: one blunt phrase repeated with small melodic variation beats a clever constructed chorus — write the one line that can loop and mean more each pass.
  - The register is numb-honest: desire, guilt, distrust, and pride sit side by side without apology; vulnerability gets admitted flatly, never dramatized — the coolness of delivery is itself the feeling.
  - Verses slide between singing and rhythmic talk — write lines that work equally well spoken and sung, because the performer will blur the two.
  - Rhyme leans rap-adjacent: internal rhyme, repeated end-words, and identical-word line endings are idiomatic here, not lazy — where 90s R&B would call that a miss.
  - Double entendres are street-smart and casual — money/love flips, city/person flips — dropped in passing, often in an opening line rather than saved for the hook, and never explained.
  - POV: guard up — the narrator states how things are more than how they hurt; the address is direct but keeps emotional distance.
  - Ad-libs are punctuation, not runs: plan single echo words and short tags after lines; long vocal runs are rare, and tone carries what runs would carry elsewhere.
  - Cross-genre firewall: hip-hop's Melodic Rap and gospel's urban/trap room run the same drums and tempo as this one — the dial that makes it Trap-Soul is the guard staying up, feelings admitted flatly instead of sung as an open ache or told as a testimony.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Dark ambient pads and detuned keys over a booming 808 sub, crisp trap hi-hats rolling in double and triple time, half-time snare. Vocals sit in reverb and delay with a subtle Auto-Tune color, doubled hooks, and murmured background ad-libs — a 2015-to-now nocturnal palette, minimal and spacious.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for situationships, late-night regret, mixed feelings, ambition tangled with love, guarded hearts, and stories the user tells in blunt fragments. Serves badly: weddings, tributes to parents, joyful celebration, wholesome family stories, or any narrator who wants to sound fully open-hearted.
- **What makes it a parody:** Forcing designer brands, substances, or city clichés the user never gave; over-singing — this style murmurs, it does not run; rhyming too neatly; letting the narrator gush emotionally when the style's whole identity is the guard staying up; cramming words into space that must stay empty.

### Quiet Storm / Slow Jam
*The grown-and-slow late-night ballad — candlelight tempo, patient phrasing, romance treated like fine dining.*

- **Tempo & groove:** Felt tempo roughly 50–80 BPM, straight or gently swung, sometimes in 6/8 (a rolling, waltz-like sway); some grooves are written faster but sway in half-time. Word density is the lowest of all siblings — every line gets room to bloom, and the words move at speaking-to-one-person pace.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Patience is the craft: one idea per section, unhurried — a verse may spend four lines setting a single scene; rushing the words is the cardinal sin of this style.
  - Address is intimate second person at whisper distance — the song is written for an audience of one, in the room, tonight, and every line should survive being said that close.
  - Sensuality is adult and elegant — suggestion over statement; the double entendre here is velvet: one ordinary domestic or natural image that turns romantic on the second listen and sustains across the whole song.
  - Every style wants singable vowels, but none holds notes as long as this one — so word choice is stricter here than in any sibling: long open vowels on the held notes, soft consonants throughout, lines that stay beautiful at half volume.
  - Vulnerability is devotional — gratitude, promise, cherishing — not wound-airing; if conflict appears at all, it resolves inside the song.
  - The climax is a vamp (the chorus loops while the lead climbs freely over it), not a bigger chorus: plan a final stretch where the chorus repeats and the lead ascends — so the chorus words must be simple enough to survive twenty repeats.
  - Rhyme lands soft and optional; a gently placed slant rhyme suits the mood better than the snap of a perfect one.
  - No hype interjections or energy filler anywhere — elegance is the register from first line to last, which no other sibling demands this strictly.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Silky electric piano with soft strings or warm synth pads, gentle drums with a rimshot snare (brushed or softly programmed), round smooth bass, optional saxophone or muted guitar. Lead vocal velvet and close-miked, restraint throughout, with the tasteful runs saved for the final vamp — mid-70s-through-90s adult late-night radio warmth.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for anniversaries, long marriages, proposals, devoted grown romance, and songs sung to a spouse or lifelong partner. Serves badly: breakups in progress, anger, casual flings, group anthems, or stories that need modern slang energy and pace.
- **What makes it a parody:** Running the seduction-cliché checklist (wine, fireplace, silk) instead of using the couple's real details; breathy overacting; letting the words rush ahead of the tempo; getting explicit — this style seduces by implication only; treating the genre as a wink or a skit instead of full sincerity.

### Classic Soul / Motown
*The 60s-70s foundation — church-born voices, live bands, and plainspoken songs that hit like testimony.*

- **Tempo & groove:** Uptempo Motown-style grooves roughly 100–130 BPM with a driving backbeat and tambourine; southern-soul ballads 55–80, often in a slow 6/8 sway. Words sit ON the beat more than any sibling — punchy, symmetrical phrases whose stressed syllables lock to the backbeat; density moderate and even.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Plain words, big feelings: the vocabulary stays simple and universal; the craft lives in stress placement and escalating repetition, not in clever wordplay — the opposite bet from Neo-Soul.
  - Lines land on the beat — write tight, symmetrical phrases whose stressed syllables hit where the snare hits, short enough for a horn section to punch between them; where 90s R&B writes longer conversational sentences that stop early to leave run-room, Classic Soul locks the words to the beat itself.
  - Call-and-response with the background voices is written into the lyric: plan short answer phrases and echo hooks for the backing trio in both verse and chorus.
  - The register is testimony — love sworn, lost, or begged for at full voice and full sincerity; irony, guardedness, and cool detachment do not exist in this style.
  - Repetition escalates: the same chorus words return hotter each time, and the outro repeats the hook while the lead preaches and improvises over it.
  - The double entendre is the church/love twin — the beloved described in near-worship language so one lyric serves both the altar and the bedroom, a tradition carried straight from gospel roots. This is also what separates the room from gospel's quartet lane: Classic Soul is secular love and celebration that borrows worship language, where Quartet-Style's engine is God's act inside a narrated story — scenes, a drive, an anchor phrase.
  - Rhyme is clean and confident — simple perfect rhymes are idiomatic here and read as timeless, where the same rhymes would read as childish in Contemporary R&B.
  - POV: full voice, testimony register — and unlike 90s R&B, the address is allowed to widen from one person to everybody listening by the final chorus, testimony becoming celebration.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** A live rhythm section — real drums, electric bass, chanking rhythm guitar (short choppy strums right on the beat), piano or organ — with horn stabs and string sweetening; tambourine on the backbeat for uptempo numbers. Raw, gospel-fired lead vocal with a background trio answering; 1960s-70s analog warmth, real room sound, no modern vocal effects.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for tributes, milestone celebrations, songs for parents and grandparents, timeless full-hearted declarations of love, and joyful dance-along family songs. Serves badly: modern situationship nuance, guarded or ironic narrators, minimal lo-fi moods, and stories that hinge on texting-era details.
- **What makes it a parody:** Fake-vintage slang and era name-dropping — the period lives in the band, the chords, and the phrasing, never in the vocabulary; over-sweetening into jingle territory; wordy verses a horn section cannot punch around; imitation-oldies pastiche instead of sincere testimony built from the user's story.

**Landing here without being told (story cues):** Read the user's story for tense, guard, and occasion. A current, messy, still-unresolved relationship told in casual everyday language (situationship, jealousy, healing, self-worth) → Contemporary R&B. Devotion at full volume — an apology, a wedding, winning someone back, wanting something big and harmonized → 90s R&B. A story with a plot — a confrontation, a confession, the night something actually happened, told as events in order — or any request for a 2000s throwback → 2000s R&B. Growth, self-discovery, spirit, gratitude, long-view love, the story is as much about who the narrator is becoming as who they love → Neo-Soul. Late-night, guarded, regret mixed with pride, ambition tangled with love, the user tells it in short blunt statements → Trap-Soul / Alt-R&B. An anniversary, a spouse, a proposal, slow grown romance meant for an audience of one → Quiet Storm / Slow Jam. A tribute, a milestone, a song for parents or grandparents, or any request that mentions old school, Motown, or throwback joy → Classic Soul / Motown. Tiebreaker for devotion stories: if the song is meant to be heard by a crowd and sung along to, go 90s R&B; if it is meant for one person hearing it up close, go Quiet Storm. An 80s-flavored party request has no exact home here — route joyful live-band family energy to Classic Soul and harder drum-machine bounce to 90s new-jack-swing, never invent an 80s costume. When unsure, default to Contemporary R&B: it accepts the widest range of stories, tempos, and emotional registers without ever sounding like a costume, and it is the register closest to how users actually talk.


## Hip-Hop

**Why the room matters here:** In hip-hop the flow IS the song — how the syllables sit against the drums matters more than what instruments play. A dense 90s pocket flow written over a trap beat sounds like a dad at a school dance; a slow sung melodic-rap flow over boom-bap drums just sounds empty. Picking the sub-genre is really picking the writing job: how many syllables per bar, where the air goes, whether the hook is chanted, sung, or barely there.

**What really separates these rooms:** Axis 1 — Flow and cadence: how words ride the drums. Boom-bap runs sentences over the bar line but stomps the rhyme word on the snare; trap fires triplet bursts then leaves air; drill slides in late and clips phrases short; melodic rap sings and holds notes; West Coast lays back behind the beat and drawls; bounce chants in short crowd-sized pieces. This is the biggest separator — same story, different flow, completely different lyric sheet. Axis 2 — Word density and space: syllables per bar and what fills the gaps. Boom-bap packs 10-14 syllables a bar and conscious goes up to 16; trap bursts 8-12 then stops; West Coast rides 8-12 relaxed; melodic rap leaves air at 6-9 so melody can live there; bounce chants shortest of all at 4-8 with crowd-response gaps. Writing dense in a spacious sub-genre or sparse in a dense one is the most common failure. Axis 3 — Hook treatment and rhyme engine: is the hook sung (melodic rap), talk-sung and cool (West Coast), chanted (trap, drill, bounce), or small and functional (boom-bap, conscious)? And does rhyme work as dense interlocking internals (boom-bap, conscious), one vowel sound ridden for many bars (trap, drill), an easy unforced lilt (West Coast), or simple repeated end-words the crowd can shout (bounce)?


### Boom-Bap
*The classic 90s sound: dusty drums, dense clever bars, and skill on display — hip-hop that rewards close listening.*

- **Tempo & groove:** 85-95 BPM. Slightly swung head-nod pocket; the kick and snare are the whole conversation and the stressed rhyme word of each bar lands square on the snare. Word density is high — roughly 10-14 syllables per bar, nearly every beat carries a word.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Flow decision: the one constant is that the stressed rhyme word lands on the snare. Sentences are free to run across the bar line — the great 90s writers did this constantly — but every bar should still be quotable on its own, a punchline that can stand alone. That quotability is what separates boom-bap from conscious rap, where sentences chain across bars to serve the plot.
  - Rhyme engine: dense multi-syllable rhyme, including internal rhyme (rhymes inside the line, not just at the end). Chain two or three rhyming syllables at a time, and change the rhyme sound every 2-4 bars so it never becomes a typewriter.
  - Wordplay placement: this is the sub-genre where flips, double meanings, and punchlines live in the VERSE, roughly one clever turn every 2-4 bars — setup bar, payoff bar. In the siblings, cleverness is seasoning; here it is the meal.
  - Hook treatment: small and functional. A short chanted refrain, a repeated phrase with a cut-up scratched-record feel, or no sung hook at all — the verses are the star and the hook just resets the room.
  - Section shape: 16-bar verses are standard, two or three of them. No pre-chorus, rarely a bridge. Each verse must open a new angle on the story — same subject, new camera position.
  - Subject treatment: everyday concrete detail is the currency — the block, the job, the small specific moments of the user's story told with wit. Skill itself is allowed to be part of the subject: the writing can visibly enjoy being good.
  - Vocal space: almost none needed. No ad-lib gaps, no melody room — the bar is full. Write wall-to-wall and trust the drums.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Dusty chopped soul or jazz sample as the main melody, thick swung kick-and-snare break with vinyl crackle, deep upright-style or filtered bassline. DJ scratches and record cuts can act as the hook. 85-95 BPM, raw close-mic rap vocal with no pitch tuning, small room feel, 90s East Coast character. Keep the beat loop-based and let it ride — no big drops or builds.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for: witty roasts, skill-flex tributes, nostalgia stories, day-in-the-life narratives, odes to a craft or a city, users who say real hip-hop or name 90s artists. Bad for: tender love songs and grief (the density crowds the feeling out), party songs meant for dancing (the pocket nods heads, it doesn't move hips), and anything that needs a big singable chorus.
- **What makes it a parody:** Forced 90s slang the user never wrote; rhyming every single syllable until the meaning dies; fake New York references for a user from somewhere else; generic nostalgia filler instead of the user's actual story; tough-guy content the story doesn't contain. Boom-bap parody sounds like a rhyme robot — real boom-bap sounds like a sharp person talking in rhythm.

### Trap
*The modern mainstream sound: booming 808 bass (deep bass-drum boom), rattling hi-hats, short punchy phrases with swagger and space.*

- **Tempo & groove:** Usually written at 130-150 BPM but FELT in half-time (the body hears roughly 65-75). The snare cracks on beat three, hi-hats roll in fast triplet bursts (three quick hits per beat). Word density is medium and bursty — quick runs of 8-12 syllables, then deliberate air where the ad-libs (the echo words between lines) live.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Flow decision: write in bursts, not full bars — a rapid phrase (often in threes, matching the triplet hi-hats), then a gap. Each phrase should be able to stand alone like a caption; long winding sentences kill the bounce. The gaps are not empty; they are where the echo and ad-lib live, so the writer must plan which word gets echoed.
  - Rhyme engine: ride ONE rhyme sound for 4-8 bars before switching, and let repetition be a device — repeating the same word at the end of back-to-back lines is a legitimate hammer, not laziness. This is the opposite of boom-bap's constantly-shifting internals.
  - Hook treatment: chanted, not sung — two short phrases traded back and forth, simple enough to say along by the second listen. The hook can open the song before any verse, and it returns often.
  - Section shape: short verses (8-12 bars), hook every 30-40 seconds, no pre-chorus, rarely a bridge. Momentum comes from the hook returning, not from a build.
  - Subject treatment: present tense, first person, confident — the user's wins, grind, loyalty, glow-up stated as facts. Vulnerability is allowed but delivered flat and unbothered; one plain line dropped between flexes hits harder than a whole sad verse.
  - Concrete detail rule: swagger only works when the details are the USER'S — their real car, job, block, win. Generic luxury inventory is how two different users end up with the same song, which is failure.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Deep gliding 808 sub-bass, crisp fast hi-hats with triplet rolls, hard clap or snare on beat three, sparse dark melody — bells, muted keys, a lone flute or pad. 130-150 BPM with a half-time feel. Modern lightly-tuned rap vocal with a stacked ad-lib track echoing key words. Wide, punchy, radio-modern mix with plenty of low end.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for: hype songs, hustle and glow-up stories, birthday flexes, gym and pregame anthems, celebrating a win, making one person feel like a boss. Handles pain too, if the user's story carries it — flat delivery over hard drums reads as strength. Bad for: tender detailed storytelling (the burst-flow chops narratives into confetti) and songs that need a big emotional sung chorus.
- **What makes it a parody:** Inventing drug, gun, or street content the user never wrote — the number one trap parody; brand-name shopping lists; writing the ad-libs into every line like punctuation; forcing triplets onto every single bar until it sounds like a typewriter; confusing loud with confident. Real trap is economical and cold; parody trap is busy and try-hard.

### Drill
*The cold, sliding, late-night cousin of trap: menacing bass that slides, clipped deadpan (flat-voiced) phrases, confidence with the temperature turned down.*

- **Tempo & groove:** 138-150 BPM, almost always right around 140, half-time feel. The signature is the sliding 808 bass (a deep bass boom that bends in pitch) and drums that hit slightly off the grid — so phrases enter LATE, behind the beat, and get clipped short. Word density medium-high but chopped: 9-13 syllables per bar delivered in short cold pieces, little air for melody, some air for punch.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Flow decision: slide in late. Where trap bursts on top of the beat, drill phrases start a hair behind it and lean on the last word of each phrase — write lines whose final word is the heaviest one, because that's the word the flow stomps on.
  - Tone dial (the big one): understatement. Drill's power is saying a hard thing flatly, like it barely matters. Exclamation is weakness here; the coldest line in the verse should read almost bored. If humor shows up, it is a dry shrug inside an otherwise serious line — never a set-up-and-punchline like boom-bap. This is the exact opposite of bounce's shouted energy.
  - Rhyme engine: slant rhyme (near-rhyme) over perfect rhyme, and repeated cadence patterns — the same rhythmic shape recurring line after line matters more than the rhyme sounds matching cleanly.
  - Hook treatment: the hook is a colder, tighter verse — same flow, same deadpan, just more repetition. The boundary between verse and hook blurs; do not write a lifted singable chorus, it breaks the spell.
  - Point-of-view norm: the crew voice is at home here — we and us carry weight, and direct address to a rival or a doubter is a standard move. Use it only if the user's story actually has a them.
  - Subject treatment for a consumer app: translate drill's edge into what the user actually wrote — rivalry, doubters, surviving something, cold self-belief. NEVER import violence, gang references, or threats the user's story does not contain.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Sliding, pitch-bending 808 bass (the defining sound), sparse eerie melody — a lonely piano, a bell, a ghostly vocal texture — and syncopated (off-beat) skittering hi-hats with hard off-grid snare placement. 138-150 BPM (usually right at 140), half-time feel. Deadpan low-energy rap vocal, dry and up-front, minimal tuning, UK/NY drill drum character. Dark, minimal, no warmth in the mix.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for: rivalry and doubters stories, cold-confidence anthems, survived-something narratives, competitive sports or business energy, users whose own words carry an edge. Bad for: celebrations, love songs, tributes, and anything warm — drill cannot smile without breaking. If the user's story has no adversary and no edge, do not put it in drill.
- **What makes it a parody:** Invented street beef or gang content — instantly fake and potentially harmful; borrowed UK slang from a non-UK user's story (the dialect law applies to slang too — only the user's own words); mistaking shouted aggression for drill's cold restraint; threats aimed at nobody. Parody drill is loud and cartoonish; real drill is quiet and heavy.

### Melodic Rap
*Half-rapped, half-sung feelings over booming soft beats — the lane for heartbreak, longing, and loyalty said out loud.*

- **Tempo & groove:** Written 130-150 BPM with a half-time feel, so it FEELS slow and floaty (roughly 65-75 in the body). Flow sings: notes get held, words get stretched. Word density is LOW — about 6-9 syllables per bar — with the most air per phrase of any rap-flow lane; melody fills what the words leave empty.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Flow decision: write phrases that can be SUNG, not just said — end lines on open vowel sounds (ah, oh, ay) that a voice can hold and bend, and keep lines short enough that stretching a word doesn't crowd the next one.
  - Rhyme engine: clean, singable end rhymes and very few internal rhymes. Dense internals fight the melody; here the rhyme's job is to make the tune feel inevitable, not to show skill.
  - Hook treatment: fully sung, and it IS the emotional thesis of the song — the one line the user's whole story boils down to. Chorus lines must match each other in syllable count and stress so the melody repeats cleanly — Layer 3's matching-length-lines rule matters most in this sub-genre.
  - Section shape: the only hip-hop sub-genre where a pre-chorus (the short build before the chorus) and a bridge genuinely belong. Verses can melt into the hook instead of stopping dead — write the last verse line to hand off, not to slam shut.
  - Subject treatment: feelings stated to a specific person — second person address (you) is the home position. Name the feeling AND the concrete moment it comes from; a feeling with no scene is greeting-card mush.
  - Vulnerability dial: this is the one hip-hop lane where soft is strong. No armor required — but the confession must be the USER'S confession, in their situation, not stock heartbreak.
  - Repetition as emotion: repeating one phrase with a different melody or emphasis reads as obsession or ache — a legitimate device here that would read as lazy in boom-bap.
  - Cross-genre firewall: R&B's Trap-Soul and gospel's urban/trap room run the same drums and tempo as this one — the dial that makes it Melodic Rap is the guard coming down, a sung ache aimed straight at a person.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Warm atmospheric foundation — ambient keys, soft guitar, or airy pads — over booming but rounded 808 bass (deep bass-drum boom) and relaxed half-time trap drums with only light hi-hat rolls. 130-150 BPM half-time feel. Heavily melodic tuned vocal with harmony stacks and background layers; the voice is the lead instrument. Emotional, spacious, late-night mix.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for: heartbreak, missing someone, love, apology, grief, loyalty, ambition mixed with loneliness — any story that is mainly a FEELING aimed at a person. The single most useful lane for a consumer app's sentimental stories that still want to knock. Bad for: wit-driven roasts, dense storytelling, and crowd-chant party songs — the low word density can't carry jokes or plot.
- **What makes it a parody:** Autopilot heartbreak clichés and numbness tropes the user never wrote; vowel-mush lines with no concrete image (pretty sound, says nothing); every line the exact same length in a singsong lull; sad-boy costume on a story that isn't sad. Real melodic rap earns the ache with one specific detail; parody just moans in tune.

### Conscious / Storytelling
*Hip-hop with a point: real narratives, tributes, and messages told with craft — the verse as a short film.*

- **Tempo & groove:** Most at home at 85-100 BPM with a live, roomy pocket — the constant is drums that leave space for words to be HEARD. Word density is the highest in hip-hop, 10-16 syllables per bar, but delivered conversationally, like gripping speech more than performance.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Flow decision: shift cadence WITHIN the verse to mark emotional turns — start conversational, tighten and speed up as tension rises, slow down for the gut-punch line. Sentences chain across bar lines in service of the plot; unlike boom-bap, no single bar has to work as a stand-alone punchline — the paragraph is the unit here, not the bar.
  - Structure decision: the story poses a QUESTION and answers it late. Somewhere along the way something must shift — a new set of eyes, a different point in time — but the user's story decides how many verses it needs and where that shift lands; never assign fixed jobs to verse numbers. Verses run long: 16-24 bars is normal.
  - The extended metaphor: this is the one sub-genre where a single comparison can carry the WHOLE song — the entire story told as if it were something else, revealed fully only near the end. In the siblings a metaphor lasts a line or two; here it can be the architecture.
  - Hook treatment: understated on purpose — sparse, low, sometimes half-spoken, sometimes just a breath between chapters. A big shiny chorus would cheapen the story; the hook's job is to let the listener absorb, then pull them back in.
  - Detail-first rule: proper nouns, dates, places, small physical facts from the user's story do the emotional work. The lesson is never stated first — the details earn it, and the point lands late.
  - Rhyme engine: internals as dense as boom-bap but in SERVICE of the argument — rhyme follows the sentence, the sentence never bends to reach a rhyme. When the honest line doesn't rhyme, the honest line wins (Layer 3's no-rhyme option lives here).
  - Point-of-view discipline: choose the narrator deliberately — first person confessing, third person telling someone else's story, or second person putting the listener inside it — and breaking that point of view once, late, is a power move, not an accident.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Live-feeling drums or a warm boom-bap hybrid, soulful keys, strings, or a gospel-tinged sample; the beat should DROP OUT or thin down under the most important lines. 85-100 BPM. Clear untuned vocal sitting close and upfront, minimal effects, dynamic arrangement that breathes with the story rather than looping flat. Sparse, low-key hook treatment.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for: tributes to a parent or a lost loved one, life-lesson stories, overcoming-something narratives, injustice, faith journeys, telling a real person's real story with dignity. The lane for the app's heaviest, most meaningful requests. Bad for: party songs, flexes, and quick fun — it takes itself seriously and needs a story with actual weight to justify that.
- **What makes it a parody:** Preaching — turning the user's story into a sermon aimed at the listener; clichés that order the listener to pay attention or get conscious instead of showing a life; rhyming-dictionary big words to sound deep; stating the moral up front instead of earning it; name-dropping social issues the user's story never touched. Real conscious rap shows a life and lets the listener conclude; parody lectures.

### West Coast / G-Funk
*Laid-back funk-driven rap built for cruising — a relaxed, talk-sung flow that leans back on a rolling groove and sounds like a good day.*

- **Tempo & groove:** 90-100 BPM, swung rolling funk bounce — the flow sits BEHIND the beat, unhurried, stretching vowels at phrase ends. Word density is medium, about 8-12 syllables per bar, but delivered so relaxed it feels lighter; nothing is ever allowed to sound rushed.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Flow decision: lay back. Phrases start a touch after the beat and drawl through it — where boom-bap stomps the snare and trap bursts on top, this flow lounges. If a line can't be said in one relaxed exhale, it's too crowded — cut words until it can.
  - Rhyme engine: easy, conversational end rhymes with a light singsong lilt — a few internals for bounce, never packed. The rhyme must sound effortless; the moment a rhyme feels reached-for, the cool breaks.
  - Hook treatment: talk-sung and smooth — melodic but cool, never aching. The hook states a vibe, not a confession; that is the line between this lane and melodic rap, which sings feelings AT someone.
  - Subject treatment: the good day — scene-setting sensory detail carries the song: the weather, the ride, the company, the music, the food, all from the user's story. Wins are told with a shrug and a smile; urgency is the one thing this lane refuses.
  - Vocal space: medium — leave the ends of phrases open so the high synth line and the bassline can answer. The groove is a duet partner, not a backdrop; write as if something else gets the last word of the bar.
  - Tone dial: wry and warm — observational humor delivered slow, never set-up-and-punchline like boom-bap. Even the flex is friendly; this is the one hip-hop lane where the confidence smiles.
  - Section shape: standard verses with a sung hook and one steady temperature throughout — instead of a bridge, leave room for the groove itself (a synth or bass break) to take a turn. No drops, no builds; the ride just keeps rolling.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Rolling funk bassline up front, a high whining synth lead that slides between notes (the classic G-funk whistle), warm electric-piano chords, and laid-back swung drums with a snappy snare. 90-100 BPM. Smooth, relaxed, talk-sung vocal with light melody and a few harmony touches, sunny 90s West Coast character. Groove-led and steady — no drops, no big builds, just ride.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for: windows-down cruising songs, laid-back celebrations, summer cookouts and reunions told smooth instead of loud, good-day stories, city or neighborhood pride with a smile, users who ask for a West Coast vibe. Bad for: urgent hype (too relaxed), heartbreak and grief (too sunny), dense wordplay showcases, and cold rivalry — this lane cannot frown for long.
- **What makes it a parody:** Fake California scenery — palm trees, lowriders, and beach references for a user whose story never leaves Ohio; borrowed 90s West Coast slang the user never wrote (the dialect law covers slang too); importing gang references from the era's famous records; forced chill — filler about relaxing instead of the user's actual good day. Real G-funk is unhurried confidence in the user's own scene; parody is a costume.

### Southern Bounce / Club
*Call-and-response party rap built for a room full of people shouting back — the celebration machine.*

- **Tempo & groove:** 95-105 BPM, straight relentless groove with a rapid syncopated (off-beat) kick — no half-time, no swing, just forward drive. The drum flavor can vary (New Orleans bounce, crunk stomp, modern club) but the writing engine is the same. Word density is the fewest syllables per line in hip-hop — short chant-sized lines of 4-8 syllables — but the lines turn over fast, so the song never sits still.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - The writing engine is call-and-response: write in pairs — a call line, then a gap or a response line a CROWD could shout back. Every section must pass the test: could a room full of people who just heard it once yell the answer? If not, shorten it.
  - Command voice: this is the one sub-genre where telling the listener what to do is the norm — commands that get the room moving, celebrating, or honoring the named person. Build the actual command wording from the user's own story and people, never from stock party phrases. Imperative (command) sentences that would sound bossy elsewhere are the native grammar here.
  - Rhyme engine: simple, percussive, and proudly repetitive — repeating the same end word across lines is not lazy here, it IS the hook mechanism. Complex internals would only slow the crowd down.
  - Hook treatment: the hook is the whole point and it eats the song — expect the hook to take up half the runtime, with mini-verses (4-8 bars) as quick breathers between chant sections. Structure is hook-heavy, verse-light: the mirror image of boom-bap.
  - Roll-call device: naming — the birthday person, the team, the family members, the city — chanted so the named people react. This is the sub-genre's superpower for celebration songs, and the names must come from the user's story (which also keeps the originality law satisfied automatically).
  - Space rule: leave the response gaps EMPTY in the writing. Filling every beat kills the party — the crowd's answer is part of the song, so the lyric sheet must have holes shaped like it.
  - Energy discipline: no dynamic dips. Where melodic rap breathes and conscious rap slows down for weight, bounce holds one high temperature start to finish — the only build allowed is stacking more voices.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Choose the drum character from the story's energy: rapid New Orleans bounce-style kicks, a heavy stomping crunk-style clap groove, or clean modern club drums — all straight and driving at 95-105 BPM. Sharp claps and a simple bass riff; horn stabs and whistle hits only when the story wants full-blast parade energy. Lead chant vocal answered by stacked group-shout response vocals — the call-and-response must be audible in the arrangement. Big live party energy, handclap layers, no ballad softness anywhere.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for: birthdays, graduations, weddings, team and family anthems, reunions, city pride, any story whose purpose is a ROOM celebrating together. The best hip-hop lane for making the named person light up in front of everyone. Bad for: introspection, heartbreak, tributes to the departed, and detailed storytelling — the chant format physically cannot hold a narrative or a quiet feeling.
- **What makes it a parody:** Generic party filler so interchangeable that any name could be swapped in (the exact failure the originality law forbids — the user's specific people and moments must be IN the chants); instructing dances that don't exist or are ten years stale; writing it as generic pop-EDM party lyrics with no call-and-response structure; filling every gap so the crowd has nowhere to answer; locking every party song into one city's sound the user never mentioned; and instructing any regional dialect — the bounce rhythm carries the identity in standard English.

**Landing here without being told (story cues):** Read the user's story for its energy and its target. Story is a feeling aimed AT someone — heartbreak, missing a person, love, apology → Melodic Rap. Grief tiebreak: raw feeling spoken TO the person → Melodic Rap; telling the story OF the person's life → Conscious/Storytelling. Story celebrates a person or a moment — birthday, graduation, team win, party → Southern Bounce if it should make a room move and shout; West Coast/G-Funk if the celebration is laid-back — a ride, a summer day, a smooth cookout or reunion; Trap if it should make one person feel like a boss. Story is about the user's own grind, wins, hustle, glow-up, proving doubters wrong → Trap. Story has a dark competitive edge — rivalry, cold confidence, coming from a hard place and unbothered about it → Drill, but ONLY when the user's own words carry that edge; never add menace they didn't write. Story is a real narrative with a lesson, a tribute to someone's life, an injustice, or a message → Conscious/Storytelling. Story leans nostalgic, values wit and skill, mentions the 90s or wants a clever roast → Boom-Bap. Common user vocabulary maps straight to lanes: old school or real hip-hop → Boom-Bap; emo rap, SoundCloud rap, sad rap → Melodic Rap; UK drill or NY drill → Drill; twerk, club, or bounce → Southern Bounce; West Coast, G-funk, or lowrider vibe → West Coast/G-Funk. The single declared default when the story gives no clear signal: Trap — it is the mainstream center of hip-hop today and what most users mean when they just say make it hip-hop. A story that is mainly a feeling sung toward another person rather than about the user themselves is a strong cue, not a default — it routes to Melodic Rap.


## Gospel

**Why the room matters here:** In gospel, the sub-genre decides who is singing and which direction the song faces — one voice testifying to people, a whole room singing straight to God, or a leader and a group trading lines. Get it wrong and the song fails its real job: a wordy personal testimony can't be sung by a congregation, and a vague worship chorus wastes the user's specific miracle. Each lane also climbs to its ending differently — the choir's key-change vamp (a vamp is a short ending phrase repeated over and over while the music climbs), worship's repetition that deepens, the quartet's drive, contemporary's production drop-and-rebuild — and since the ending is the most important part of a gospel song, the wrong pick makes the whole song fall flat exactly where it should soar.

**What really separates these rooms:** Axis 1 — Direction and voice: is this a testimony "I" told TO people about what God did, a corporate "we/You" sung directly TO God, or a lead voice and a group in call-and-response (leader sings, group answers)? This one choice rewrites every line on the page. Axis 2 — Who must be able to sing it: a congregation or choir needs short, square, repeatable phrases learned in one pass; a soloist showcase wants longer conversational lines with air left for runs (fast vocal note-flurries) and off-beat phrasing. Axis 3 — How the song ascends at the end: stacked vamp (a short repeated ending phrase) plus a key change (choir), the same phrase repeated over a growing build until it means more (worship), a narrative drive with an escalating lead over one hammered group phrase (quartet), or a production drop-out and rebuild (contemporary/urban).


### Traditional Gospel / Choir
*The big-choir church sound — a leader out front, a wall of voices answering, and an ending that climbs higher than the beginning.*

- **Tempo & groove:** Two lanes: slow burn at 60-75 BPM in a 12/8 feel (a triplet sway — the classic slow-church rock), and uptempo praise at 100-126 BPM, straight or lightly shuffled with claps on 2 and 4. Word density is low-to-medium: every choir line must be short enough for forty voices to hit together. Slow songs stretch few words over long bars; uptempo songs use punchy call phrases.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Write two layers at once: the LEAD's lines (freer, testimony-flavored, can stretch and repeat) and the CHOIR's answer (short, fixed, 3-7 words a room can lock onto). Every section should say which layer carries it.
  - Build the vamp on paper — the vamp is the repeated ending section. The craft is choosing THE one short phrase worth repeating 8-16 times while intensity climbs, plus a second short phrase (the 'special') that stacks on top of or answers it near the peak.
  - Rhyme density is low. Repetition and parallel structure (matching line shapes) do the work rhyme does in other genres; a choir anthem can rhyme almost nothing and still land.
  - POV convention: the verse may testify in 'I,' but the chorus widens to 'we' or turns to 'You/He' — the whole room has to be able to claim the chorus words as their own.
  - Scripture allusion is welcome and expected — folded in as borrowed images and familiar turns of thought, never chapter-and-verse citations dropped mid-line.
  - The section plan is different from pop: verse → chorus → vamp → (often) key change → reprise (the chorus comes back one more time). The vamp usually replaces the bridge; if a bridge exists, its job is to set up the key change.
  - Keep lines metrically square (even, landing on the beat) and predictable — a choir cannot syncopate loosely the way a soloist can. Put the surprise in harmony and dynamics, not in rhythm.
  - Write the dynamics into the lyric arc: place the softest, most confessional line right before the biggest lift — it should be NEW information, carried by the lead voice alone, giving the director a near-whisper to climb from.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Full mass choir in three-part harmony behind a powerhouse lead vocalist, Hammond B3 organ with Leslie speaker (classic church organ), grand piano, live bass and drums, tambourine on uptempo numbers. Slow songs in a 12/8 sway with big choir swells; uptempo with handclaps on 2 and 4. Call for a key change into the final vamp and a drum-led drive ending. Era marker: rooted in the 1970s-90s mass-choir tradition and still recorded live today — live church-recording energy, warm and roomy, not polished pop.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for church celebrations (anniversaries, pastor appreciation), big communal gratitude, deliverance stories meant to be shared by a congregation, and homegoing services. Bad for intimate private confession and stories built on lots of specific narrative detail — the choir format compresses detail out, and whatever specifics survive must live in the lead's verse lines only.
- **What makes it a parody:** Cramming long wordy lines into choir parts (a real choir cannot sing a paragraph together). Stuffing in fake old-church vocabulary the user never wrote. Skipping the vamp — a traditional choir song that simply ends after the second chorus sounds like an unfinished demo. Starting loud and staying loud, leaving the ending nowhere to ascend to.

### Contemporary Gospel
*Modern radio gospel — a soloist's personal testimony with pop/R&B polish, a big singable chorus, and a bridge that lifts the whole song.*

- **Tempo & groove:** Ballads 58-72 BPM (often in 6/8 — a rolling, waltz-like sway — or a half-time feel, where the drums move at half speed), mid-tempos 84-104 BPM with an R&B-leaning pocket, celebratory uptempos 108-126 BPM. Word density is medium: verses conversational and specific, choruses tightened into short parallel lines. Phrasing sits between choir writing and urban gospel — some push and pull against the beat, but the melody stays the star.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Testimony 'I' carries the whole song more than in any other gospel lane except quartet — the singer's specific story IS the song; the chorus universalizes the feeling without abandoning first person.
  - Split the specificity: verses hold whatever hard specifics the user actually wrote — put those in, unsoftened; the chorus states the resolved truth in plain, singable words, with one short echoable phrase inside it that background voices can lift and answer. Specific verse, universal chorus.
  - Rhyme density medium and mostly slant (near-rhyme) — perfect rhymes reserved for chorus landing lines; in the verses, honesty outranks rhyme.
  - The bridge is load-bearing: it holds the turn (the moment faith kicked in, the decision, the answered prayer) and typically climbs melodically into a final key-lifted or re-voiced (sung a new way) chorus.
  - Leave run room: end key phrases on open vowels and leave a beat or two of air at line ends for the vocalist to fill — but less ad-lib room (ad-libs are short unscripted vocal extras) than urban gospel takes.
  - A late-song vamp is optional and shorter than a choir vamp — usually the chorus's single strongest phrase, isolated and repeated 4-8 times with ad-libs floating over it.
  - Scripture allusion is lighter-touch here: a familiar biblical image folded into modern everyday speech, not quoted phrasing.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Polished modern production: piano or keys-led, live-feeling drums, electric bass, clean guitar swells, string or pad beds, and tight stacked background vocals (a small polished stack, not a mass choir) entering at the chorus. Lead is a featured soloist with gospel runs. Ballads big and cinematic with a key lift near the end; mid-tempos with an R&B-informed pocket and lush extended church chords. Era marker: 2000s-2020s gospel radio — clean, warm, contemporary sheen over church harmony.
- **Stories it serves:** The widest lane in gospel. Best for personal testimony of any kind (health scares, grief, recovery, new beginnings, gratitude), encouragement songs aimed at one struggling listener, and milestone tributes carrying a faith frame. Weak for purely corporate worship moments (a room singing to God belongs in Praise & Worship) and for stories that want grit and street texture (urban gospel serves those better).
- **What makes it a parody:** Stacking greeting-card faith clichés with zero personal detail. Choruses so wordy nobody could sing along by the second listen. Skipping the turn — a testimony with no before-and-after is just a mood, not a testimony. Forcing churchy vocabulary onto a user who wrote their story in plain modern speech.

### Praise & Worship
*Songs a whole room sings straight TO God — simple enough to learn in one pass, deep enough to repeat for ten minutes.*

- **Tempo & groove:** Worship ballads 60-76 BPM with a straight feel and long sustained phrases; anthemic builds 72-84 BPM; uptempo corporate praise 100-128 BPM straight-ahead. Word density is the LOWEST in gospel: few words, long notes, lots of air. A congregation learning the song in real time cannot process dense lines.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - POV is vertical and constant: second person TO God ('You') dominates, with 'we/us' for the room. Personal 'I' appears only as the doorway into 'You.' This is the one gospel lane where you do NOT pivot POV mid-song — the whole song faces one direction, and switching from talking TO God to talking ABOUT God breaks the room.
  - Write for instant singability: phrases of roughly 4-8 syllables, repeated exactly, easy to pitch. A first-time listener should be singing by the second chorus. Predictability is a feature here, not a flaw.
  - Depth comes from repetition that deepens, not from new information: the same phrase means more the fifth time because the build and the room have grown around it. So choose phrases that can bear repetition — declarative, present-tense statements about who God is that stay true every time they're sung, not one-time narrative events.
  - Rhyme density is very low — heavy rhyme can even feel manipulative in worship. Parallel phrasing and repetition replace it.
  - Structure convention: low, personal verse (the approach) → chorus (the declaration) → a bridge or tag that becomes THE moment — in modern worship the short escalating bridge is often the most-repeated, most-remembered part of the song. Design it as the peak, not an afterthought.
  - Pick ONE attribute of God that the user's actual story points at — provider, healer, protector, comfort — and stay on it the whole song. Listing many attributes flattens all of them.
  - Write a line that can survive being whispered: the standard arc includes a near-silent breakdown (voice plus one instrument) before the final full-band chorus. Unlike the choir lane — where that quiet moment is a NEW confessional line from the lead — the exposed line here is the SAME declaration the room has been repeating, proving it still holds with nothing behind it.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Two textures live in this one lane — pick by the user's story and references. The default for this genre is gospel worship: piano and Hammond organ leading, rich church-chord harmony, choir-flavored group vocals swelling behind the lead — warm, live sanctuary energy. Switch to the ambient worship-band texture (warm synth pads, delay-and-reverb electric guitars, a steady kick-driven build, roomy congregation-style group vocals, a close and plain lead) only when the user's language or references point there. Run density follows the room the user describes: gospel worship leads run freely; the ambient band sound keeps the lead close and plain. Either way, keep the long dynamic arc — sparse first verse, near-silent drop-down, huge layered final choruses. Era marker: 2010s-2020s live-recorded worship, spacious, builds over flash.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for stories written as prayer or thanks addressed to God, congregational moments (services, baptisms, a worship moment inside a wedding), and seasons of surrender, awe, or waiting. Bad for detailed personal narratives (worship compresses story into a single posture toward God), tribute songs to people, and any request where the user's specific events are the point — this lane trades specificity for a room full of voices.
- **What makes it a parody:** Vague stock nature imagery stacked with no anchor in the user's actual story. Wordy verses nobody could sing on first hearing. Pivoting mid-song from addressing God to describing God. Building a huge emotional swell around a line that isn't worth repeating — the build must be earned by the phrase at its center.

### Urban Gospel / Inspirational R&B & Trap
*Faith with today's sound — gospel truth over R&B and trap production, written in the user's everyday voice.*

- **Tempo & groove:** R&B-leaning cuts 60-80 BPM with a half-time bounce and deep pocket; trap-influenced tracks written at 130-150 BPM but felt in half-time (so they groove like 65-75); mid-tempo bounce 88-100 BPM. Word density is the highest in gospel — conversational, syncopated phrasing with more syllables per bar than any sibling lane, but still leaving pockets of air for ad-libs.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Write in the user's actual speech register: modern, casual, plain. Church vocabulary appears only where the user's own story already lives there — the faith is in the content, never in costume word choice.
  - Testimony 'I' with the mess left in: whatever hard specifics the user actually wrote go into the verses unsoftened. The before must be as vivid as the after — sanitizing the struggle kills this lane.
  - Syncopation is the engine: start phrases off the beat, tuck extra syllables, land early or late. The words should bounce like an R&B or hip-hop melody line, not sit square like a hymn — this is the one gospel lane where square phrasing is the mistake.
  - Rhyme density is the highest in gospel: internal rhyme and multi-syllable slant (near-rhyme) borrowed from hip-hop are welcome — but conversational flow still outranks rhyme when they conflict.
  - Hooks behave like R&B hooks: short, rhythmic, repeated — often an everyday phrase flipped so it carries a second, sacred meaning at the same time. Double meanings are at home here in a way they are nowhere else in gospel.
  - Leave ad-lib real estate: pockets at line ends for stacked ad-libs, spoken asides, and breathy runs — the space IS part of the writing.
  - The ascent is a production move, not a key change: write one nakedly honest lyric moment specifically for the beat drop-out before the final hook, where the voice stands alone.
  - The bridge may be spoken or rapped — a talking-to-yourself or under-the-breath prayer moment reads authentic here where it would break the other gospel lanes.
  - Cross-genre firewall: R&B's Trap-Soul and hip-hop's Melodic Rap run the same drums and tempo as this one — the dial that makes it Urban Gospel is testimony, a before-and-after with an ascent at the end.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** 808 bass, trap hi-hats with rolls and stutters or smooth R&B drum programming, dark-to-warm keys and pads, pitched vocal chops, tight modern vocal stacks with heavy ad-lib layering. Lead vocal intimate and close-mic'd; light saturation or melodic autotune texture acceptable as a style choice. Half-time bounce with a beat drop-out before the final hook. Era marker: mid-2010s to 2020s gospel-R&B and Christian trap crossover — playlist-ready, mixed like mainstream R&B/hip-hop, church chords hidden inside the production.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for younger voices, testimonies with rough edges (streets, recovery, anxiety, hustle seasons), encouragement for people who don't sit in pews, and faith-rooted motivation. Bad for congregational singing (no room sings a syncopated melody together), church-service moments, and older-generation tributes where the production choice itself would read as disrespectful.
- **What makes it a parody:** Renting slang the user never wrote — borrowed slang ages in a week and reads fake instantly. Preaching down at the listener over a trap beat — this lane talks WITH people, never at them. Scrubbing the struggle until the song is all victory lap. Porting church clichés onto modern production without translating the language.

### Quartet-Style (Southern Soul Gospel)
*The old-school quartet sound — a gritty lead telling a story while the group answers behind them, driving harder and harder until the church is on its feet.*

- **Tempo & groove:** Slow-burn story songs 66-84 BPM with a heavy swung backbeat. The drive (the intensifying ending push) escalates intensity and often pushes the tempo forward — but some drives stay at the original tempo and climb through dynamics and the lead's density alone. Almost always swung, never stiff. Word density splits: lead verses are medium-dense and narrative — nearly talked — while the group's answer lines are tiny, 2-5 words hammered on the same beat every time.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Write it as a story with scenes: quartet verses are narrative — a specific day, a specific place, what happened, who said what. This is the most story-forward lane in gospel; the user's concrete details go IN, not compressed out. This engine — God's act inside a narrated story, told through scenes, a drive, and one anchor phrase — is also what separates the lane from R&B's Classic Soul, which is secular love and celebration borrowing worship language.
  - Put two voices on the page: the lead's lines (long, conversational, preacher-adjacent phrasing that can stretch and repeat at will) and the background answer (ONE short fixed phrase the group repeats identically — choose it early, because it anchors the whole song).
  - The DRIVE is the ascent: the final section strips down to the anchor phrase while the lead escalates over it. Write the anchor plus three or four escalation ideas — turns and intensifying restatements of the same thought — instead of new verses.
  - Repetition-as-preaching is the craft: the lead repeating one line three or four times with growing intensity is the style working, not lazy writing — a rule that would be wrong in nearly every other lane.
  - Rhyme is loose and optional; the storytelling cadence and the call-and-response lock (leader calls, group answers on time) matter far more than rhyme.
  - POV: testimony 'I' or third-person parable — telling somebody else's story to make the point — are both traditional. Direct-to-God 'You' address is rare in this lane.
  - Physical, lived-in, and concrete beats abstract: pull the two or three most touchable details out of the user's own story and build scenes around those.
  - A spoken or half-sung opening is a legitimate section: the lead 'talking' the audience into the song before it fully starts is part of the tradition.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Electric guitar with twang and tremolo, bass and drums with a heavy swung backbeat, Hammond organ or piano stabs, four- or five-voice harmony backgrounds behind a rough-edged lead vocal — grit, rasp, and shouts welcome. The arrangement must build to a drive: tempo and intensity climbing, backgrounds chugging one phrase, lead ad-libbing over the top, hard hits and holds at the finish. Era marker: rooted in the 1950s-70s quartet tradition and still alive on today's Southern soul gospel circuit — live, raw, church-floor energy over studio polish.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for narrative testimonies told start to finish, tributes to elders and mothers and fathers, songs for older congregations, homegoing services, and long-road stories spanning years of struggle or family history. Bad for abstract feelings with no events, youth-targeted songs, and quiet intimate confessions — this format is public and communal, built for a room, not headphones.
- **What makes it a parody:** Faking country-church grammar or spelling out an accent the user never wrote — the rhythm and phrasing carry the tradition in standard English. Writing polished, symmetrical pop lines for the lead, who should read like heightened speech. Skipping the drive and ending like a pop record. Making the group's answer a full sentence — if the background phrase can't fit on a church fan, it's too long.

**Landing here without being told (story cues):** Read who the user's story is addressed to. If it is written TO God — thanks, prayer, surrender, awe — choose Praise & Worship. If it is a personal before-and-after testimony told to other people (sickness, grief, recovery, a season of doubt that turned), choose Contemporary Gospel — or Urban Gospel/Inspirational if the user's own voice is casual and modern, keeps the mess and grit in (streets, anxiety, hustle, relapse), or they ask for R&B/trap sounds. If the song is FOR a church event, a choir, a pastor appreciation, an anniversary, or a big shared celebration, choose Traditional Gospel/Choir. If the story is a detailed start-to-finish narrative, honors elders or older congregations, or carries a Southern/country flavor, choose Quartet-Style. Homegoing (funeral) songs: Traditional or Quartet if celebratory and communal, Contemporary ballad if intimate and personal. If the user asks for 'something like a hymn' (common from older users and for funerals), use Traditional's slow lane: the slow swaying feel, short even lines, low rhyme. Name warning: to many users 'Southern Gospel' means the smooth, piano-led, country-flavored convention sound — polished, blended four-part harmony, no grit. That is NOT the Quartet lane here; give those requests a clean verse-chorus shape and square singable lines over country-leaning instrumentation, not a gritty drive ending. Route out when gospel is the message but not the sound: gospel over Afrobeats or reggae grooves belongs in the Reggae/Afrobeats genre with gospel's direction-of-address and ascent thinking carried over, and a whole-song rap testimony belongs in the Hip-Hop genre with gospel's testimony and ascent thinking applied — Urban Gospel here only holds a rapped bridge, not a full rap song. Safe default when unsure: Contemporary Gospel — it holds any personal story, fits any occasion, and never reads as the wrong choice for a testimony.


## Reggae / Afrobeats (two families, one priority slot)

**Why the room matters here:** This slot holds two families and six very different lanes. A roots reggae song and a dancehall song share an island but almost nothing on the lyric sheet — one is a slow communal chant, the other a dense, bouncing boast in the present tense; same with afrobeats versus amapiano, where one is a sweet melodic love phrase and the other is barely words at all. And every lane here shares one instant-death mistake: machine-written dialect. Pick the wrong lane and the song is not just off — it reads as a costume, which in these genres is worse than being boring.

**What really separates these rooms:** Axis 1 — Word density and where the silence goes: dancehall verses are dense and rap-adjacent; roots and lovers rock use short breathing phrases; afrobeats uses tiny repeatable melodic cells; amapiano uses a handful of chanted lines and long vocal-free stretches. Each lane strikes a different deal between the words and the groove, and honoring that deal IS the genre. Axis 2 — Who the song is for: the whole community (roots, amapiano), the party in front of you right now (dancehall), or one specific person (lovers rock, afrobeats romance, alté). This decides point of view, hook style, and how personal the details get. Axis 3 — How repetition works: a crowd chant that gains power each round (roots, amapiano), a private phrase re-sung with new melodic bends (afrobeats, lovers rock), or one rhyme sound ridden hard for momentum (dancehall). Repetition is a feature in all six lanes — but a different feature in each.


### Roots Reggae
*The classic 1970s reggae of faith, struggle, and uplift — slow, deep, and sung like a message to a whole people, whether the news is heavy or good.*

- **Tempo & groove:** 70-90 BPM in a one-drop feel (the drum's big hit lands on beat 3, leaving beat 1 empty), with swung offbeat guitar chops. Word density is low-to-medium: short phrases with real air between them so the bass and the offbeat guitar stay audible.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Widen the story: the singer's personal pain or hope is treated as everyone's — a lost job becomes a song about a system, a sick mother becomes a song about endurance, one person's good news becomes everyone's reason to smile. Keep the user's specific details in the verses, but let the chorus speak for more than one person.
  - The hook is a chant: a short rallying phrase of roughly three to six words, built for a crowd to sing together, repeated more times than feels polite on paper. It gains power each round.
  - Phrasing starts late and ends early: begin lines just after the downbeat and stop before the bar ends, so the offbeat guitar and the bass melody get the spotlight between phrases.
  - Rhyme loose: repeating a key word beats a clever rhyme; slant rhyme and no-rhyme are normal; a tight, showy rhyme scheme reads as the wrong genre.
  - Imagery is big and physical — pulled from the nature, weather, work, and places in the user's OWN story, never from a stock reggae image bank. It may lean scriptural only if the user's own story carries faith. Never postcard island scenery.
  - Sections: verses may run long and preach a little; the bridge is often replaced by a stripped-down instrumental stretch (a dub drop) where the words stop entirely — plan the lyric around that silence.
  - Point of view (who is speaking, to whom): an 'I' that stands for 'we,' or direct address to the powerful or to the people. Second-person confrontation is fully at home here.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** One-drop drums with kick and rim landing together on beat three, deep melodic bass mixed up front, offbeat guitar skank, bubbling organ, horn-section stabs, spring-reverb echo trails. Either 1970s analog tape warmth or the cleaner modern roots-revival sound. Lead vocal answered by a small harmony group singing the responses.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for: struggle, injustice, faith, endurance, homeland, family legacy, warnings and hope — and just as naturally the light side: easy communal joy, gratitude, good news, a carefree day, reassurance for someone who is worried. Roots carries heavy and light with the same warm, communal voice. Poor fit: flirty club stories, petty gossip, luxury bragging — those belong to dancehall.
- **What makes it a parody:** Any patois spelling the user did not write themselves; marijuana references the user never mentioned; religious names or slogans inserted uninvited; postcard island imagery (beaches, palm trees, cocktails); borrowing famous reggae hook phrases; making the chorus clever instead of chantable.

### Lovers Rock
*Reggae's tender love-song lane — soft, romantic, and sung close, born in late-1970s and 80s UK and Jamaican studios.*

- **Tempo & groove:** 65-80 BPM, a gentle swung one-drop with every edge softened. Low word density with long held notes at line-ends; the singer croons, so lines must leave room to stretch and bend.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Keep the world small: one room, two people. Never widen to community or message — that is roots territory. The whole song is a private conversation.
  - The verses are a real conversation moving forward: verse two must advance the story of these two specific people — an answer, a memory, a next step — never re-circle verse one's feeling. This linear two-person story is exactly what an afrobeats love song does NOT do (it circles one feeling), so the two lanes can never produce the same sheet.
  - Write for a crooner: end the lines that carry the feeling on open vowels a voice can hold and slide; avoid hard consonant endings there.
  - Couplets that resolve: gentle perfect or near-perfect rhymes that close sweetly feel romantic here, where roots deliberately leaves lines hanging open.
  - The hook is a promise or a question aimed at one specific person — intimate, never a crowd chant. If a stadium could shout it, it is wrong for this lane.
  - Answer vocals are part of the writing: backing voices echo the last words of lines, so write line-endings worth echoing.
  - The bridge earns its place with a confession or a doubt — the one moment the sweetness admits a crack, which makes the final chorus land harder.
  - Point of view: first person to 'you,' and that 'you' must feel like the real person from the user's story — include one detail only they would recognize.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Soft one-drop drums with a rimshot instead of a full snare, warm round bass, silky offbeat guitar, electric-piano chords, lush strings or pads, a sax or melodica fill answering the voice. Tender lead vocal with airy doubled harmonies and generous reverb; late-70s/80s London-studio warmth.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for: love letters, anniversaries, apologies, new crushes, missing a partner, devotion through hard times. Poor fit: protest, party bangers, boasting, explicit heat — lovers rock suggests, it never spells out; that heat belongs to dancehall.
- **What makes it a parody:** Confusing tenderness with beach-bar island-romance clichés; getting sexually explicit; machine-added patois pet names; a chorus that behaves like a crowd chant instead of a whisper; strings-and-roses greeting-card lines with no specific person in them.

### Dancehall
*Jamaica's high-energy party and boast music — born in the late 1970s, defined by the mid-80s digital era, running to today's chart crossovers: punchy digital riddims and rapid rhythm-riding vocals.*

- **Tempo & groove:** 95-110 BPM with the signature syncopated bounce (kick hits grouped 3+3+2 across the bar — a long-long-short bounce). Verses are word-dense and rap-adjacent; hooks are short chants or commands. The groove bounces, so the words must bounce with it.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Verses ride the riddim (the instrumental backing track): near-rap cadence with dense internal rhyme locked to the bounce — the highest word density anywhere in the reggae family.
  - Ride one rhyme sound for four or more lines on purpose: hammering the same end-sound builds momentum here, where in most genres it would read as lazy.
  - The hook is a command or a catchphrase: tell the floor what to do or state the boast flat. Two short alternating lines trading back and forth make a legitimate hook.
  - Write punch-and-pause: land a hard phrase, then leave a beat of silence for the crowd's reaction — the space after the punch is part of the lyric.
  - Present tense only: the song happens NOW, in the party or on the road. No backstory, no reflection — energy over narrative, always.
  - Sections contrast by flow, not by chords: the riddim barely changes, so switch the vocal rhythm between sections instead. Verses run short (around eight bars), the hook returns often, and the pre-chorus is usually skipped.
  - Boasts are stated, never explained — the moment a boast gets justified, it dies.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Hard digital drums with the 3+3+2 syncopated kick, cracking snare or clap, heavy sub bass, one dark minor-key synth riff doing most of the melodic work, sparse arrangement with hype vocal doubles on the punch words. Delivery is rhythmic deejay-style (Jamaican talk-singing, an ancestor of rap) more than melodic singing — delivery-style descriptors like that are allowed, but render tags must never request dialect words or accent spellings beyond what the user's own sheet contains; modern versions may add trap-style hi-hats.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for: party anthems, confidence and swagger, dance, bold flirtation, rivalry and winning, hustle pride. Poor fit: grief, worship, tender apology, slow reflection — the groove has no patience for them.
- **What makes it a parody:** Machine-written patois is the single fastest way to make it fake; American rap phrasing pasted over the beat (loses the bounce entirely); long storytelling verses; explaining or softening the boast; hooks with too many words for a crowd to shout back.

### Afrobeats
*The melodic West African pop sound of the last fifteen years — sweet, groove-heavy, low word count, built for repeat listening and dancing.*

- **Tempo & groove:** 95-115 BPM (most hits sit 100-110), swung percussion with the vocal sitting slightly behind the beat, relaxed. Very low word density: short melodic phrases, heavy repetition, and open space in every bar for the percussion to talk.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Melody-first economy: phrases of roughly three to seven words, each strong enough to be sung four times in a row without boring anyone. Fewer, better words.
  - Repetition is the architecture, not a failure: the hook can be half the song; freshness comes from how the melody bends on each repeat, not from new words. The song circles ONE feeling instead of moving a story forward — plot belongs to other lanes.
  - End phrases early: leave the last beat or two of each bar empty — that gap is where the shakers, vocal echoes, and groove live. A full bar of words is a mistake here.
  - Choose words for sweetness of sound: open vowels, soft consonants, words that glide. A plain line that sings smooth beats a clever line that crowds the mouth.
  - Joy can sit inside anything: gratitude, blessings, and celebration mix naturally into love songs and success songs — thankfulness is a home register, never a clash.
  - Wordless melodic syllables are legitimate hook material as texture the story rides on — but they can never replace the user's actual story.
  - Sections blend: verse, pre, and chorus flow at similar energy; the chorus lifts through melody and stacked voices, not by getting louder or wordier.
  - Point of view: simple and direct — first person to a lover, to God, or to life itself. Keep the address uncomplicated; complexity belongs to alté.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Mid-tempo swung drums with a syncopated kick, tight shakers, rim and woodblock-style percussion, warm rounded bass, airy plucked guitar or bell-like synth riffs, wide soft pads. Silky lead vocal with a light modern tuning sheen, stacked harmonies and echo answers; current Lagos pop polish.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for: love and desire, celebration, gratitude, weddings, glow-ups, missing someone across distance, soft-life joy. Poor fit: dense storytelling, anger, protest detail, any story that needs a complicated plot — the word budget is too small.
- **What makes it a parody:** Machine-written pidgin is the number-one killer; overstuffed lines that smother the groove; fake African-language exclamations dropped in for flavor; rewriting every repeat as if repetition were a flaw; safari or jungle imagery (an offensive cliché, not local color).

### Afro-fusion / Alté
*The moodier, more personal cousin of afrobeats — alté is Lagos slang for 'alternative' — blending African rhythm with R&B, dancehall, and indie feeling, made for night drives and complicated emotions.*

- **Tempo & groove:** 80-110 BPM with freer feels — half-time moods (drums feel half speed), dancehall-leaning bounces, and slow R&B burns are all allowed. Word density is medium and deliberately shifts between sections; phrasing is looser and more conversational than mainstream afrobeats.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Interior over communal: sing the specific private feeling even when it is awkward or contradictory — where afrobeats keeps things sweet and universal, this lane rewards the odd true detail.
  - Conversational phrasing: lines can run long and land off the grid like a thought spoken out loud; symmetry between lines is optional, not required.
  - One strange, true image per section: a specific sensory detail from the user's story — a place, an object, a time of night — does more than any pretty generality.
  - Flow-switch between sections on purpose: a verse can move like dancehall while the chorus floats like R&B. Write the sections at different word densities to force that switch.
  - Hooks can be moods, not chants: a hushed repeated phrase works fine here; it never needs crowd energy.
  - Rhyme relaxed and slanted; honesty outranks rhyme every single time in this lane.
  - Ambition and its cost is a home subject: wanting more and what the wanting takes from you — a register mainstream afrobeats rarely touches.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Hybrid production: afrobeats percussion softened under R&B chords, hazy guitars or lo-fi synth textures, a dancehall-tinted bounce or half-time trap-touched drums, air and space in the mix. Intimate close-mic vocal with light tuning, moody reverb, late-night atmosphere; the feel of the Lagos alternative scene from the mid-2010s to now.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for: complicated love, self-discovery, homesickness, grief that still grooves, ambition versus peace, faith and doubt. Poor fit: straight-ahead party anthems, wedding-crowd singalongs, message music for a community.
- **What makes it a parody:** Mistaking moody for vague — it still needs one concrete story; machine-written pidgin; pasting sunshine afrobeats clichés into what should be a night song; endless atmosphere-words with no specific feeling underneath them.

### Amapiano-influenced (Afro-piano)
*The South African dance sound, now pan-African, built on the deep bouncing log drum — hypnotic, spacious, and chant-driven rather than verse-driven.*

- **Tempo & groove:** 108-118 BPM (most songs sit 110-115), rolling and hypnotic with long builds. The lowest word density in this entire slot: short chant phrases, call-and-response cells, and long stretches where the voice steps aside completely.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Write chants, not verses: one- and two-line cells designed to loop, stack, and layer. A whole song may need only a handful of distinct lines used many different ways.
  - The drop belongs to the log drum (the deep, bouncy signature bass): plan sections where the vocal thins to a single repeated word — or full silence — when the bassline takes over.
  - Build call-and-response into the writing itself: a lead phrase, then a gap sized for a group answer — and write the answer line too, don't leave it to chance.
  - Pick percussive words: consonant attacks that lock with the shakers. The lyric works as another drum, so mouth-feel outranks meaning-density here.
  - Structure is a slow build, not a verse-chorus seesaw: the main hook may arrive late; energy accumulates over minutes — write for patience, not for a quick payoff.
  - Keep sung phrases short and clipped; long vocal runs fight the groove. One soulful line floating over the top is a feature; three is a traffic jam.
  - Compress the user's story to its emotional core — one victory, one feeling, one name — because there is no room for plot, only for the feeling of the moment.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Rolling groove built on the signature log drum bass, soft jazzy piano chords, tight shakers and crisp hi-hats, airy pads, long filtered builds and breakdowns. Sparse lead vocal chants with layered group answers and an occasional soulful sung phrase floating over the groove; South African house lineage, 2020s club sound.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for: celebration, victory laps, dance-floor release, friendship and crew love, shaking off stress, gratitude in motion. Poor fit: heartbreak ballads, detailed storytelling, confessional intimacy — there are simply too few words available for a complex tale.
- **What makes it a parody:** Delivering a full, dense lyric sheet (instantly wrong); fake South African-language exclamations; treating it as afrobeats at a different tempo — the space rules are much stricter; leaving no room for the instrumental drop; hooks too wordy for a crowd to chant back.

**Landing here without being told (story cues):** Read the story's register (its emotional temperature) first. Struggle, injustice, faith, endurance, or a message meant for a community → Roots Reggae. A simple happy story with a reggae feel — good news, a birthday, a carefree day, gratitude, reassurance for someone who is worried → also Roots Reggae, on its sunny side: same groove, lighter words. A tender love story, apology, anniversary, or devotion with a reggae feel → Lovers Rock. Party energy, boasting, dance, bold flirtation, or rivalry → Dancehall. Sweet love, celebration, gratitude, or success with a modern African pop feel → Afrobeats. Complicated, moody, personal — mixed feelings, the cost of ambition, late-night introspection → Afro-fusion / Alté. Pure dance-floor celebration where the feeling matters more than the plot → Amapiano-influenced. The Reggae family's single declared default is Roots Reggae — it carries message, struggle, and feel-good stories alike — and a romance story is a strong cue that routes to Lovers Rock instead. The Afro family's single declared default is Afrobeats — the widest, safest lane in the family — for when the user says only "afrobeats" or gives an African-pop-flavored story with no other signal. Never default to Dancehall or Amapiano; both punish stories that need narrative room. Routing note: reggaeton and soca do NOT live in this slot — a reggaeton request re-routes to Pop's Latin/Tropical lane, which exists today, and soca belongs with carnival party music; Dancehall stands in only when the user has locked the genre to Reggae, and even then treat it as a compromise, never a match.


## Pop

**Why the room matters here:** Every pop sub-genre agrees the hook is king, but they disagree on everything else: how many words fit in a line, where the title sits, how polished versus conversational the lines should sound, and whether the feeling arrives on the beat, on a held note, or in a small detail. A wedding story written as dance-pop sounds careless; a birthday story written as a ballad sounds like a funeral. Pick the wrong sub-genre and the user's real feeling gets read as the WRONG feeling — that is worse than a weak song.

**What really separates these rooms:** Axis 1 — Word budget versus pulse: how many syllables the tempo allows per line and how much empty air the beat demands. Dance-pop is the sparsest (short punches with gaps), singer-songwriter pop is the densest (the lyric IS the production), and Latin/tropical pop sits in between, riding the syncopation (notes landing between the beats). Axis 2 — Polish versus talk: whether lines should sound engineered and perfect (dance-pop, ballad) or overheard and conversational (bedroom pop, singer-songwriter). This includes rhyme: perfect bright rhymes in the polished lanes, slant (close-but-not-exact) or no rhyme in the talky lanes. Axis 3 — Where the emotion is delivered: on the body and the beat (dance-pop), on the long held note (ballad), in the cool atmosphere (synth-pop), in the tiny true detail (indie/acoustic), at shout volume (pop-punk), or in the sway of the groove itself (Latin/tropical). The same story lands in a completely different place on each.


### Dance-Pop
*The big-night-out pop song — built to move bodies first and hearts second.*

- **Tempo & groove:** 100-128 BPM, kick drum on every beat, straight feel. Low word density: short punchy phrases with real air between them so the beat can carry. Verses are lean; choruses are leaner.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Hook economy is extreme: the chorus must be singable after ONE hearing. The title opens the chorus and usually closes it too — announced, not arrived at.
  - Phrasing runs short (roughly four to seven syllables a line) with gaps; a single repeated word can legitimately be a whole line here and nowhere else in pop.
  - The pre-chorus is a physical ramp, not an emotional one: lines get shorter and the word-pace quickens as it climbs, so the body feels the lift coming.
  - Rhyme is clean, bright, and mostly perfect, landing on open vowels — the mouth has to land easy at speed. Clever slant rhymes read as drag here.
  - Subject treatment compresses time: present tense, tonight, this room, this moment. The backstory gets one line, never a verse. If the story needs a timeline, this is the wrong lane.
  - Plan the post-chorus (the section after the chorus where the beat pays off): it needs its own tiny vocal idea — one short phrase or a wordless singable shape — decided at the writing stage, not left to production.
  - Point of view is first person to a 'you' who is physically present; direct address and short, simple command verbs aimed at someone in the room feel natural here — teach the verb type from the user's own story, never a stock list.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Four-on-the-floor kick (kick drum hitting on every beat) with sidechained synth bass (bass that ducks each time the kick hits), bright plucked synths, claps, and risers into every chorus. Polished lead vocal with tight doubles and shiny stacked backgrounds. Modern club-pop sheen (2010s to now), or add disco strings and octave bass for a retro flavor. For celebration stories that want a live band instead of a club, flex to a funk flavor — live drums, popping bass, choppy rhythm guitar, horn stabs — with the same writing dials.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for celebrations, birthdays, nights out, confidence anthems, new-crush excitement, and the dance-floor moment of a wedding. Serves badly: grief, apologies, complicated histories — anything that asks the listener to sit still and follow a story.
- **What makes it a parody:** Cramming a full narrative into the verses; stacking party-scene cliché props instead of the user's own moment; writing a chorus of long clever sentences (dance-pop choruses die of cleverness); forgetting the post-chorus so the drop arrives with no vocal hook on it.

### Pop Ballad
*The slow, big-feeling pop song — the one that plays at the wedding or makes the whole room go quiet.*

- **Tempo & groove:** 60-80 BPM, straight feel or a gentle 6/8 sway; drums often enter late and in half-time (hitting half as often, so everything feels bigger). Moderate word density: the slow tempo buys longer lines, but the peak moments must be left open for held notes.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - The title leans toward landing late — at the end of the chorus or on its held peak, earned by the lines before it — where dance-pop leans toward announcing it up front. This is a lean, not a law: if the user's own key phrase wants to open the chorus, let it lead. What matters is that the title feels arrived at, not chanted.
  - Verses are storytelling rooms: full sentences, longer lines, and more concrete detail from the user's story than any other polished-pop lane allows.
  - The pre-chorus works emotionally, not physically: it names the stakes and often gets QUIETER — instruments drop out rather than pile on — so the chorus can bloom.
  - Dynamics are the architecture: verse one nearly spoken, chorus one restrained, final chorus wide open. Write chorus lines that survive being sung both whispered and huge, because they will be.
  - The bridge is load-bearing: the confession, the decision, or the turn lives there. A ballad whose bridge just restates the chorus louder is a loop, not a journey.
  - Rhyme relaxes: slant rhyme (close-but-not-exact) is welcome and an honest unrhymed line beats a forced perfect one — at this speed the melody covers the seams.
  - Plan the money note: end the key chorus lines on open vowels (ah, oh, ay) because the singer will hold them for bars. Do not pack so many words into the peak that there is no room to hold and bend a note.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Piano or clean guitar foundation; strings or warm pads swell in by the second chorus; drums arrive late with big half-time weight. Intimate close-mic vocal early, stacked harmonies late, and optionally a final-chorus key lift. Timeless adult-pop palette — 90s diva ballad through modern piano-pop.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for love declarations, weddings, grief and tributes, apologies, parent-and-child songs, anniversaries — any heavy or ceremonial feeling. Serves badly: jokes, hype, casual fun; ballad treatment makes small feelings sound overblown and insincere. Routing tiebreaker with Acoustic/Singer-Songwriter: if the user gave a detailed timeline they want heard word-for-word, send it there; if the feeling is bigger than its details and wants a held-note ceremonial moment, it belongs here.
- **What makes it a parody:** Every line at maximum emotion so there is no arc left to climb; greeting-card abstractions replacing the user's one concrete scene; a bridge that adds volume but no new information; burying the held-note moments under piles of syllables.

### Synth-Pop / Retro-Wave
*Neon 80s-flavored pop — moody synths, a driving pulse, and feelings watched through a windshield at night.*

- **Tempo & groove:** 85-125 BPM — the lane spans moody-slow to driving, and the steady even grid from the synths and drum machine, not the number, is what defines it. The groove is a steady grid, not a bounce. Medium-low word density: even, measured phrases that ride the grid — hypnotic and controlled rather than pushed and pulled.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Mood outranks plot: take the single strongest image or feeling in the user's story and circle it for the whole song, instead of walking through events in order. This is the one pop lane where NOT advancing the story much is correct.
  - Phrasing is even and gridded: lines of matching length delivered coolly on the pulse; the ragged conversational spill that works in bedroom pop breaks the trance here.
  - Understatement is the emotional engine: the surface stays cool and the ache lives in what is held back. Belting (singing it big and loud) is a lane violation — restraint IS the feeling.
  - The chorus can be a short repeated phrase — usually the title — that returns like a chant and gathers weight each pass rather than lifting; dance-pop would demand more shape here, and a ballad more payoff.
  - Rhyme is moderate and often slant (close-but-not-exact); hard, clever, snappy rhymes puncture the atmosphere.
  - Point of view is first person reflective, speaking to an absent 'you' — the person is in the memory, not in the room. That distance is the sub-genre's signature and should shape verb tense: lean past and conditional (would, could) over present.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Repeating analog-style synth bass pattern, 80s drum-machine character with a big gated snare (that huge chopped-off drum sound), shimmering pads and arpeggios (chord notes played one at a time), optional chorused electric guitar. Vocal sits back in space with reverb, delivered cool. Aim at a 1983-1986 palette or its modern retro-wave gloss.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for nostalgia, longing for someone far away or long gone, bittersweet memories, late-night solitude, and stylish self-possession. Serves badly: warm cozy domestic stories, comedy, and any story that needs a detailed sequence of events told in order.
- **What makes it a parody:** Name-checking the decade — listing 80s props and references instead of sounding like the era; overheated belting that breaks the cool; cramming syllables against the steady grid; letting stock neon-and-midnight boilerplate stand in for the user's own images.

### Indie / Bedroom Pop
*Small-sounding, personal pop — like a voice memo from someone you have a crush on.*

- **Tempo & groove:** 70-105 BPM, laid-back straight or lightly swung feel, sitting slightly behind the beat. Conversational word density: lines can run long and spill over like real speech, then land hard on a short one — the unevenness is the style.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Lead with the user's smallest, most particular true detail — inside the first two lines, not saved for the bridge — and let that one detail carry the emotion a ballad would hand to a key change. Understatement is law: the biggest feeling gets the plainest line, and this lane rewards the user's oddest true detail more than any other pop sub-genre.
  - Phrasing mimics talk: uneven line lengths, run-on thoughts, mid-line pauses. Metrically perfect matched lines — mandatory in dance-pop — read as fake here.
  - The hook is an off-hand phrase, not a banner: it should sound discovered, like something actually said, and the title often hides mid-chorus instead of opening or closing it.
  - Rhyme density is the lowest in pop: mostly slant (close-but-not-exact) or none. An obvious locked rhyme scheme reads as trying too hard in this room.
  - Sections blur on purpose: the pre-chorus is often skipped, the chorus may be barely bigger than the verse, and the whole song can sit on two chords — so the words alone must create the shape and the turn.
  - Point of view is first person and self-aware; dry humor and correcting yourself mid-line feel natural here. Address the 'you' like a text message, never like a proclamation.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Soft drum machine or lazy live kit, warm round bass, jangly or lo-fi guitar and keys; tape hiss and wobble are welcome, not flaws. Intimate close vocal with minimal tuning sheen and light doubling. The production should feel homemade on purpose — the 2015-to-now bedroom aesthetic or 2000s indie-pop warmth.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for crushes, quiet heartbreaks, friendship, anxiety and self-talk, and small everyday moments the user wants made meaningful. Serves badly: grand declarations, ceremonies, and hype — big-occasion energy collapses this small room.
- **What makes it a parody:** Performative quirkiness (random odd objects inserted for quirk's sake instead of drawn from the user's story); vague mumbly lines with no actual detail — lo-fi is a sound, not an excuse to say nothing; bolting on a big polished chorus lift that belongs in dance-pop; stacking sad-kid clichés instead of one honest specific.

### Acoustic / Singer-Songwriter Pop
*One voice, one guitar or piano, one true story told straight — the coffeehouse confessional that can still fill a stadium.*

- **Tempo & groove:** 70-110 BPM, straight or gently swung, with the strum or fingerpicking pattern carrying the groove itself. Highest word density in pop: the lyric IS the production, so lines hold more detail and more syllables per bar than any sibling lane.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Narrative is the spine: verses move through time — then to now — with scenes, people, and places. This is the ONE pop lane where a story with a beginning, middle, and turn genuinely fits; use the user's timeline instead of compressing it.
  - Every line is exposed: with no production to hide behind, a filler line that would slide by in dance-pop stands out naked. Cut anything that only exists to reach the rhyme.
  - The title is a meaning-shifter: the same chorus words should land differently each time because the verses in between changed what they mean. That re-lighting trick is this lane's signature move.
  - Rhyme is craftsmanlike and noticed: internal rhyme and tight schemes are welcome here, because listeners in this lane hear rhyme skill as care — the opposite of bedroom pop's allergy to it.
  - Let plain-spoken lines outnumber the image lines: the plain talk is what makes the images land. Wall-to-wall imagery reads as a poetry exercise, not a song.
  - Build the dynamics into the words: since no synth stack will lift the chorus, write chorus lines that shorten and quicken to create lift, and let the bridge go QUIETEST — the near-whispered admission before the last chorus — rather than biggest.
  - Address is across-the-kitchen-table: second person to a real, named-in-the-writer's-mind person; the more the app knows who is being spoken to, the better this lane writes.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Acoustic guitar (fingerpicked or strummed) or felt piano front and center; brushed drums, soft bass, maybe light strings arriving late. Dry, present lead vocal with minimal effects — natural breaths kept in. Timeless palette: it could pass for 1971 or 2024.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for tributes, family stories, life milestones, gratitude, honest heartbreak, and any story where the user gave rich detail they want heard word-for-word. Serves badly: party energy, dance moments, and swagger — this lane cannot fake adrenaline. Routing tiebreaker with Pop Ballad: a detailed timeline the user wants heard word-for-word belongs here; a feeling bigger than its details, built for a held-note ceremonial moment, belongs in the ballad.
- **What makes it a parody:** Greeting-card sincerity — abstract virtues instead of the user's actual scenes; rhyming so densely it turns sing-song; nonstop imagery with no plain-spoken landing lines; adding drops or post-choruses that betray the intimacy the arrangement promised.

### Pop-Punk / Pop-Rock Anthem
*Loud, fast, grinning pop with guitars — the shout-along for feelings that need volume.*

- **Tempo & groove:** 150-190 BPM for pop-punk (verses sometimes feel half-time — drums hitting half as often), 120-140 BPM for the pop-rock anthem end. Driving straight eighth-notes (a fast, even chug) with a hard backbeat (the snare cracking on beats 2 and 4). Word density flips by section: rapid-fire wordy verses, then a chorus that drops to short shoutable chunks.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - The chorus must survive being yelled by a crowd: short declarative lines, simple enough for a room to catch on first listen. All nuance gets exiled to the verses — the reverse of the ballad, where the chorus holds the deepest line.
  - Verses are motormouth: fast, wordy, hyper-specific, often funny or self-deprecating. The speed of detail is the charm; slow scenic description kills the engine.
  - Attitude is a writing ingredient: exaggeration, sarcasm, and blunt honesty are natural here. The bridge is usually where this style drops the joke and lets the real feeling show for a few lines before the last chorus — but that is the lane's habit, not a rule: if the user's story leads with sincerity, the writing follows the story.
  - Rhyme runs punchy and dense: snapping couplets with hard end rhymes; at this speed even simple rhymes feel athletic, and slant-rhyme subtlety just sounds like a miss.
  - Write the gang-vocal moments in: plan which word gets echoed by the group and which line gets answered (call-and-response — one voice states, many voices reply). That is a lyric decision, not a production afterthought.
  - Leave one passage nearly wordless for the crowd-shout melody moment this lane expects — plan where it goes, but never let wordless filler BE the hook.
  - Subject treatment converts pain to defiance: the breakup becomes a victory lap, the anxiety becomes a battle cry. If the user's story stays sad with no fight in it, route it to a ballad instead — this lane at this speed makes plain sadness sound sarcastic.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Distorted power-chord guitars, driving live drums with a big cracking snare, melodic bass runs. Energetic, slightly raspy lead vocal with gang vocals answering on the chorus. Either 2000s pop-punk crunch or modern pop-rock polish with a few synth touches.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for breakups the user is over, quitting the bad job, underdog wins, friendship anthems, growing-up nostalgia, and funny revenge energy. Serves badly: tender tributes, fresh grief, and romance still in progress — volume flattens tenderness.
- **What makes it a parody:** Faking teen-angst vocabulary when the user's story is clearly adult; stacking generic rebellion props instead of the user's real grievance; choruses built of complex sentences nobody can shout; playing genuinely sad material straight at 170 BPM so it accidentally reads as mockery.

### Latin / Tropical Pop
*Warm, swaying pop with a rolling Latin groove — made for dancing close, not jumping.*

- **Tempo & groove:** 88-105 BPM over a dembow rhythm (the rolling boom-chick drum pattern under most reggaeton) or a lighter tropical bounce. The groove leans off the beat rather than landing square on it. Medium word density: phrases ride the syncopation (notes landing between the beats), with real space left for the rhythm to answer back.
- **How the writing changes:**
  - Phrases push and pull against the beat instead of landing on it: write lines that invite the singer to lean in early or drag late, where dance-pop wants the words square on the grid.
  - The hook is a loop, not an announcement: a short phrase that circles back again and again, gathering heat each pass — closer to a chant you sway to than a banner you shout. Dance-pop announces its title; this lane circles it.
  - Repetition is welcome at a level no other pop lane allows: repeating one line or even one word several times in a row builds the trance here, where most other pop lanes would call it lazy.
  - Subject treatment is sensual slow-motion: the song lingers on one person, one dance, one night, noticing small physical details from the user's story — the opposite of a story that travels through time. Romance and flirtation are the home keys; warm joy and celebration fit too.
  - Rhyme leans on repeated vowel sounds more than hard end rhymes: the same open vowel ringing at the ends of lines (assonance — vowel rhyme) suits the melody's roll better than snapping consonants.
  - Plan the second voice: which words get echoed and which lines get answered (call-and-response — one voice states, another replies) is a lyric decision made at the writing stage, and here the answer voice flirts rather than shouts.
  - Language law: Spanish — or any language beyond English — appears ONLY if the user wrote it in their own story. The groove and the syncopated phrasing carry the flavor in standard English; sprinkled borrowed words the user never said read as costume, not identity.
- **How it renders (Suno production prompt):** Dembow drum pattern or a soft tropical kit, warm deep bass, syncopated piano or guitar stabs, marimba- or steel-drum-flavored synth plucks, airy pads. Smooth melodic lead vocal with light echo answers and a second voice replying on the hook. Modern tropical-pop or reggaeton-pop polish, 2015 to now.
- **Stories it serves:** Best for romance, anniversaries, honeymoon and vacation memories, flirtation, and warm celebrations that want sway instead of jump. Serves badly: grief, anger, and stories that need a detailed timeline — this groove's job is to hold one moment, not to travel through events.
- **What makes it a parody:** Sprinkling Spanish words the user never wrote (the fastest way to sound like a costume); stacking beach-and-cocktail props instead of the user's own moment; writing square on-the-beat lines that fight the groove; treating the lane as dance-pop with different drums — the phrasing must actually swing off the beat.

**Landing here without being told (story cues):** Read the story's occasion and temperature before its topic. Celebration words (party, birthday, night out, dance, we made it) point to Dance-Pop — and if the celebration wants a live-band bounce instead of club synths, keep Dance-Pop's writing and flex its funk rendering flavor. Heavy or ceremonial feeling (wedding, passed away, miss them, thank you mom, anniversary) points to Pop Ballad. A long detailed story with names, places, and a timeline the user clearly wants heard word-for-word points to Acoustic/Singer-Songwriter Pop. Nostalgia, memory, someone far away or long gone, or night-drive moods point to Synth-Pop/Retro-Wave — but an 80s mention counts ONLY when it is about the sound or mood the user wants (an 80s vibe, 80s music, synthwave, retro); a date that merely appears in the story (born in the 80s, met in the 80s) is a story fact, not a sound request, and must not pull the song out of its true lane. Small everyday moments, a crush, texting, self-doubt, or a wry self-aware tone points to Indie/Bedroom Pop. Anger with a grin — the over-it breakup, quitting the bad job, underdog defiance, or any mention of punk or rock energy — points to Pop-Punk/Pop-Rock. A tropical or Latin feel — the user asks for reggaeton, a Latin or island sound, or names a Latin hit as the vibe — points to Latin/Tropical Pop, and so does romance that wants to sway close rather than jump. When unsure: the single declared default is Dance-Pop at the gentle end of its range (around 100-110 BPM, bright but not club-hard), because that mid-tempo polished sound is what most people mean when they just say 'a pop song.' A heavy or ceremonial feeling is a strong cue, not a default — it routes to Pop Ballad. Never default to Pop-Punk, Synth-Pop, or Latin/Tropical Pop — all three are strong flavors that read as a deliberate choice.
