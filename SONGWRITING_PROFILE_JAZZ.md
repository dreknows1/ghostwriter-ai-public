## How a Jazz writer thinks

A jazz writer starts from the standard — the song-shape the whole tradition is built on, most often AABA across thirty-two bars: an eight-bar phrase, its repeat, a bridge that lifts and turns the harmony, then the last A come home changed. The title tends to land at the top or tail of the A and to name the whole feeling in a breath; the bridge is where the writing earns its keep, moving the emotion somewhere the A could not reach before handing it back. So the craft is architectural before it is verbal — build the frame, place the turn, and leave the melody room to be sung, not crammed. A lyric packed wall-to-wall, with no air for the phrasing to lag and no chorus left for a solo, has failed before its first image is judged.

The second thing a jazz writer knows is that phrasing sits behind the beat. The voice does not march the barline; it leans back, drags the phrase, holds a vowel late and lets it trail — the swing and the ache both live in that lag. So feeling-lines end on open vowels a singer can stretch, and the last beat or two of a phrase stays open, because something answers there. That answer is the third law: the instrumental solo is a real section, not an interlude. A chorus — often the bridge — is handed to the muted trumpet, the tenor sax, or the piano to state the melody and improvise over the changes, and in the up-tempo lane the voice and the horn trade fours like sparring partners. The page cannot script the improvisation, but it must build the room for it — the written gap, the solo chorus, the trade.

And the words themselves are sophisticated — interior imagery, one precise adult detail turned slowly, interior and slant rhyme surfacing on the second listen, understatement over announcement. The rooms bend all of this. The ballad slows toward rubato and croons one ache close, a muted horn answering. Swing springs up-tempo, scats, and trades fours, witty and worldly. Cool drops to a smoky 3 a.m. hush and says the least it can, phrases floating late over the widest air. The law above every dial holds: lyrics are English, and the writer's craft words — swing, changes, bridge, scat, modal, behind the beat — are tools, never the song; they never enter the lyrics, adlibs, or render notes. Delivery is directed as phrasing, tone, and swing in plain English — lean it back, hold it, let it trail — and the user's own people, places, and words outrank every stock lounge trope.

## What disqualifies a Jazz song

- No standard shape: a lyric with no 32-bar standard architecture — AABA or ABAC, the two common forms — no bridge or turn that moves the feeling, no title landing the whole ache in a breath — is a generic verse-chorus pop or lounge lyric wearing a jazz coat, not a jazz song.
- No solo section: a song that never hands a chorus to the muted trumpet, tenor sax, or piano to improvise over the changes — or an up-tempo number with no trade-fours — has cut out the event the whole tradition drives toward, and disqualifies itself.
- No room to phrase behind the beat: every bar packed wall-to-wall with words, the vowels clipped to the barline, no air for the voice to lag and hold and no gap for the horn to answer — jazz phrasing is planned around its silences, and a sheet with no air is not jazz.
- Lounge cliché instead of craft: moon-June-cocktail postcard furniture, smoky-bar greeting-card lines, generic finger-snapping patter standing in for the user's real, specific, interior detail — cliché-jazz is parody, whatever the trio does behind it.
- Craft terms in the song: swing, changes, bridge, scat, modal, or behind the beat appearing in the lyrics or adlibs — these are the writer's vocabulary, and a sheet that sings them has confused the tool for the song.
- Wrong lane: a moaned three-line AAB blues song, or a backbeat gospel-testimony soul number with a riffing horn section — those are their own genres (though a 12-bar blues FORM played swung, with jazz changes and a horn solo, stays jazz); jazz is the 32-bar standard (AABA or ABAC) with behind-the-beat phrasing and the solo written in as a section.
