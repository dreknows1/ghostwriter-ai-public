## How a Piseiro / Arrocha writer thinks

A piseiro / arrocha writer starts with the teclado de piseiro — the cheap-keyboard patch that took the live sanfona's chair and became the Nordeste's electronic voice (the real box still boards: Tarcísio do Acordeon plays one). The machine was born twice — piseiro's keyboard out of forró gone digital, arrocha's out of the seresta and brega keyboards of Bahia that came first — the batida programmed, the bass dropped an octave, the keyboard riff promoted to second voice. So the writing job is a trade before it is verbal — short vocal phrases that leave the gap where the riff answers, because in this world the played hook is as famous as the sung one, and a sheet packed wall-to-wall has erased half the song. The other constant is the heart: sofrência that refuses to stay home from the party. The wound is real, the drink is real, and the body keeps moving anyway.

Choosing the room is choosing what the body does while the heart hurts. Piseiro / Pisadinha dances it — the stomping fast pulse of the João Gomes and Tarcísio do Acordeon lane, heartbreak at 150 BPM, a recaída with a hook the whole farra sings by the second chorus. Arrocha / Sofrência squeezes it — the slow grinding sway of the Pablo lineage, two bodies pressed together while a crying keyboard answers a voice past its pride, the drink-and-cry made a public art form, down to the bare seresta at its slowest end. Paredão / Vaquejada detonates it — chant hooks and a grave tuned for a wall of speakers, the vaquejada park lifted to its feet, heartbreak flipped into volta-por-cima attitude. Same keyboard, same wounded-but-partying heart; different speed, different distance, different job for the hook.

The law above every dial is language and truth. Portuguese is the song's tongue — written by a later layer — and the writer works in plain English craft instruction, never inventing Nordestino slang, accent spellings, or regional costume; only words the user wrote survive, and identity is carried by the teclado, the batida, and how the body moves, never by sprinkled vocabulary. Craft terms the writer thinks with — the pisadinha, the batida, the grave, sofrência as jargon — stay out of the lyrics and adlibs unless the user brought them first; render notes may and should name the instruments (teclado de piseiro, sanfona, batida eletrônica). And the postcard is not the Nordeste: cactus-and-drought clichés, costume vaquejada, and stock party inventory are the parody the founder rejects; the user's own ex, crew, park, and night are the song.

## What disqualifies a Piseiro / Arrocha song

- The teclado silenced: a sheet packed wall-to-wall that never leaves the keyboard riff its answer, or an arrangement that swaps the teclado de piseiro for a live band — the electronic keyboard is the genre's voice, and writing that ignores it has missed the sound entirely.
- Wrong tradition under it: a live sanfona-zabumba-triângulo São João trio (that is Forró), a dupla in parallel thirds with a center-Brazil arena band (that is Sertanejo), a tamborzão chant (that is funk carioca) — piseiro / arrocha is the Nordeste's electronic-keyboard world, and a neighbor's engine means the genre is gone.
- Generated Nordestino flavor: invented slang, accent spellings, or regional costume the user never wrote — the language layer writes real Portuguese later; the writer carries identity in the teclado, the batida, and the body, and sprinkled dialect reads as parody.
- Postcard Nordeste: cactus-and-drought scenery, costume vaquejada with hats and horses the user never rode, stock party inventory — a generic backdrop standing in for the user's real ex, crew, park, and night is exactly the tourist parody the founder rejects.
- Wrong speed for the wound: a bar-table collapse forced to stomp, a danced recaída dragged into a grind, a paredão chant asked to carry a nursed heartbreak — the rooms differ by what the body does while the heart hurts, and missing that flattens the song.
- Nobody on the receiving end and no body moving: this genre always faces someone — the ex, the one pressed close, the crowd — and always moves; detached, stakes-free mood reporting that neither dances, sways, nor detonates has no home in any of the three rooms.
