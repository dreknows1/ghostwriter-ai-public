## How a Hip-Hop writer thinks

A hip-hop writer thinks in bars. A bar is one measure of drums plus the words that ride it — the unit of thought, the way the sentence is for prose. Before line one the writer picks a flow: how many syllables sit on the bar, where they cluster, where they stop, which syllable stomps the snare. The same sentence can become a boom-bap punchline, a trap burst, or a sung phrase depending on how it rides — flow, not subject, is what separates the rooms. Every line takes the read-aloud test against its drums: if the stresses land nowhere, it is a poem, not a rap.

The verse is the star. Sixteen bars is the standard verse and the hook usually runs eight or less, so the meaning lives in the verses. Every verse must earn its bars: a new angle, new information, the story moved forward — verse two is never verse one reworded. The hook's job is smaller and exact: state the thesis in a form a listener can carry, then reset the room. Whether it is chanted, sung, talk-sung, scratched, or nearly absent is the room's call.

Internal rhyme is the engine, and density is a dial. Rhymes inside the line, not just at its end, give rap its motor. One room chains multi-syllable internals and changes the sound every few bars; another rides one vowel sound for eight bars and hammers repeated end-words; another wants easy rhymes that sound like talking. Slant rhyme is everyday equipment everywhere. The genre-wide law is smaller and firmer: the honest word outranks the rhyming word, and a sentence bent to reach a rhyme has it backwards.

Wordplay is the genre's signature intelligence. The flip — the expected thing said the unexpected way — the double meaning with both meanings intended, the punchline built on a setup bar: all home equipment here. Where cleverness lives is the room's call — in one room it is the meal, in most it is seasoning — but the manner is a law: never announced, never explained. Every flip is built from the user's own details; a borrowed punchline is theft, a generic one is worse.

Cadence is dynamics. A hip-hop writer switches flows the way other genres change chords — tighten as tension rises, drop to half speed for the line that matters, flip the pattern at the turn. The writing also plans its own performance: gaps between phrases are written on purpose, because that is where the ad-lib cast lives, and the cast is a room decision — one room doubles its own punchlines, one is trailed by its own ad-lib track, one gets a crew's cold echo, one hands half the record to a shouting crowd. A writer who fills every gap has erased the second performer.

What disqualifies is the same list in every room: words with no relationship to the drums; starved verses under an oversized hook; identity worn as a costume. Slang, accents, place names, menace, or street content the user never wrote is the loudest parody in the genre — rhythm and cadence carry the identity in the user's own plain language. And a lyric two different users could both receive — stock flexes, brand lists, off-the-shelf struggle — has failed before the beat drops.

## What disqualifies a Hip-Hop song

- No flow: the words have no relationship to the drums — stresses land anywhere, nothing rides a pocket, and reading it aloud produces prose. If the syllables do not ride the beat, it is a poem with drums behind it, not hip-hop.
- Starved verses: the hook eats the song while thin verses repeat themselves — in hip-hop the verse is the star, and a verse that adds no new information has failed (the chant room bends the balance on purpose, but even its short verses must carry the user's real people and details).
- Costume identity: slang, accents, place references, street content, or menace the user never wrote — rhythm and cadence carry hip-hop identity in the user's own language; faked vocabulary and imported danger mark the song as parody.
- Interchangeable swagger: stock flexes, brand-name inventories, and off-the-shelf struggle any user could receive — if the wins, names, places, and details are not the user's own, the song fails the originality law before style is even judged.
- Nothing to photograph: bars built from floating abstractions with no place, object, or physical fact — hip-hop runs on concrete detail, and a rap you cannot picture is a greeting card with drums.
- Melody carries everything: a fully sung song with no rapped or cadence-led bars anywhere is R&B or pop wearing rap production — even the genre's most melodic room keeps a rap engine under the tune.
