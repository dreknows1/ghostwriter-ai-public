## How a Soul writer thinks

A soul writer starts with the feeling worn at full sincerity and the voice that carries it — a gospel-trained lead over a live band, with a backing trio answering as a second character. Soul is not cool; irony and guardedness belong to other genres. The song is aimed at someone real — a lover, an ex, a family member, the room, the God-shaped ache behind the love — and every line should survive being sung to that face with the chest open. The subjects overlap heavily — love, devotion, heartache, grief, joy — so the subject can never tell you how to write; the dials do.

The genre's oldest habit is leaving room for the voice and the answer. The lead is the lead instrument, and the words must get out of its way: key lines end on open vowels the voice can hold, bend, and break on, and the backing trio needs gaps to answer into. Where the space goes is the room's call — snapped short for a horn stab, stretched long for a moan, or opened into a Rhodes pocket — but a lyric that leaves no space anywhere has already failed as soul. Call-and-response is structural, not decoration: plan the trio's answers into the lyric, and plan the outro vamp where the hook loops and the lead preaches freely over it.

Repetition is a feature, and it escalates or deepens rather than merely repeating — the same hook returns hotter or truer each pass, so hook words must be simple and honest enough to survive many rounds. Rhyme runs on a spectrum the room sets: clean perfect rhyme reads as timeless in the vintage rooms and as an outsider's tell in the neo-soul one. The genre-wide law is smaller and firmer: the honest word outranks the rhyming word, every time, and plain words carry big feeling.

The gospel-rooted double meaning — the beloved described in near-worship language so one lyric serves altar and bedroom — is soul's birthright; use it when the user's story offers a true second meaning, never as decoration, and never announce it. Delivery is directed as feeling, register, and phrasing in plain English — the moan, the testify, the drift, the breath — never as an accent or a phonetic spelling, and never a craft term. Rendering protects three things in every room: space for the voice, a live human band and low end the phrasing was written to sit on, and the backing trio treated as its own instrument. Every dial bends to the user's story; none may change what the song is about.

## What disqualifies a Soul song

- No room for the voice or the answer: every bar packed wall-to-wall with words, leaving no space anywhere for the moan, the run, the trio's response, or a held note — if the vocal cannot breathe and nothing can answer it, it is not soul.
- No live band and no gospel-rooted delivery: a programmed 808 bounce on a quantized grid with a cool sung line and no testifying, no trio, no horns or Rhodes over a played groove — that is modern R&B wearing a soul coat, not soul.
- Nobody on the receiving end: no lover, family, room, or the God-shaped ache behind the love being addressed — detached, stakes-free mood reporting has no home in any of the three rooms.
- Cool detachment where sincerity belongs: an ironic, guarded narrator who never opens the chest and never wants anything — soul is sung open, and a song that keeps its heart hidden is not soul, whatever the band plays.
- Blues costume pasted on: a 12-bar turnaround with a slide guitar or harmonica trading the moan instead of the church-born band and backing trio — the ache in soul rises through gospel, not a blues lick.
- Slang or vintage dialect the user never wrote, sprinkled to sound soul — the era and the identity live in the band, the chords, and the phrasing, never in faked vocabulary; pasted-on costume marks the song as parody, not soul.
