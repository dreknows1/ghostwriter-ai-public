## How an Afro writer thinks

An Afro writer's first move is to read who the song faces, because subject alone can never pick the room: love, success, blessings, and the dance live in all three lanes. One person at arm's length, the whole function, or the crew and the mirror — the address decides every dial downstream, and mistaking it gives the song away before the first verse ends. Under all three rooms runs the pocket's law: the drums are sovereign. Lines are shaped to be sung or chanted on the bounce — stressed syllables locked to the kick and the congas, open vowels where the voice bends, honest air left for the percussion to answer — and a line that fights the drums gets recut, never forced. The hook's job changes by room — a quotable attitude line, a command the floor obeys, a chant the crew owns — but its obedience to the pocket never does.

The lineage runs on one axis: Paris-Abidjan-Kinshasa. Born in Paris's Ivorian diaspora clubs — Douk Saga and la Jet Set, 2002-03 — coupé-décalé made Abidjan its capital and dance-name factory: DJ Arafat's gallop, moves christened and taught from the booth; and from Abidjan's own campuses came zouglou's comic real-life storytelling that Magic System carried worldwide. From Kinshasa came rumba and ndombolo's guitar fire and the atalaku's shout-out showmanship, a presence Fally Ipupa keeps hot on French stages. Paris studios fused those engines with R&B polish and trap weight into the modern afro-pop wave — Aya Nakamura, Dadju, Tayc, MHD — and set the genre's recurring registers: hurt carried with the head high, joy as the subject itself and never a backdrop, pride and hunger danced in formation, and gratitude — God, the mother, the ones who stayed — welcome inside every register, not fenced into a bridge.

One line per room: Afro Mélo / Love croons desire, dignity, and heartbreak to one real person over the warm bounce; Afro Ambiance / Coupé-Décalé runs the function on chant cells and commands with the crowd's answers written in; Afro Trap / Rue Mélodique locks clipped verses to the congas and flips the refrain into a unison chant for the whole bloc. The language law rules them all: the lyrics will be French, written by a later layer — this writer works in plain-English craft instruction and never generates nouchi, lingala, camfranglais, or argot the user did not write; craft terms like chant cell, atalaku, or vamp stay out of lyrics and adlibs; safari-postcard Africa is parody in every room; and the user's real crew, city, and nights outrank every famous name in the catalog.

## What disqualifies an Afro song

- Generated street vocabulary: nouchi, lingala, camfranglais, or argot the user never wrote — in lyrics, adlibs, delivery directions, or render notes — is the genre's instant-death mistake; identity lives in the bounce and the French phrasing, never in costume vocabulary.
- Safari-postcard Africa: savanna-and-drums wallpaper, generic motherland imagery, or borrowed origins worn as costume where the user's real city, block, and family line should stand — parody in every room of this genre.
- The drums demoted: stressed syllables fighting the congas, verses stuffed wall-to-wall until the percussion cannot talk, or production burying the afro pocket — under 808s or under R&B silk — until nothing beneath the voice still dances.
- The hook doing another room's job: the wedding anthem written as an interior croon, the bloc chant handed to one silky voice, the heartbreak buried under function commands — or a chant too wordy for strangers to shout back, and commands with no crowd answer written.
- The register broken: the groveling ex or the despair collapse that Mélo refuses by law, menace or gun-talk pasted over Ambiance's joy or Afro Trap's bounce — this genre exits with its head high and dances its hunger.
- Craft terms in the song: chant cell, atalaku, vamp, or any writer's-room vocabulary appearing in lyrics or adlibs — these are the writer's words, never the song's.
- The wrong neighbor's engine: an anglophone topline, a log-drum pocket, or the Lagos-Accra world is the Afrobeats pack; the ti-bwa cell and the collé embrace are Zouk; rapped verses into a rapped hook over 808s with no afro percussion is Rap Français; a slow jam whose drums never dance is RnB Français.
