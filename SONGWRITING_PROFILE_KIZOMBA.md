## How a Kizomba writer thinks

A kizomba writer starts with the distance. This music was born for two bodies moving as one, and every line has to fit between them — the genre is sung at embrace distance, so the writing is warm, quiet, and close before it is anything else. Under it runs the slow pulse: an unhurried 4/4 whose kick carries a soft ghosted pickup into the downbeat, the heartbeat cell the dance walks on, the body's slow sway riding it. The job is rhythmic before it is verbal: phrases settle onto that pulse and end before the bar does, leaving the guitar, the keyboard, or the beat itself room to answer. A sheet packed wall-to-wall has broken the embrace before its first word is judged.

The lineage is Angolan and it matters. Semba — Luanda's storytelling party music, Bonga's world — absorbed the drum machines and the zouk records the French Antilles sent across in the 1980s, and Eduardo Paim's generation grew kizomba out of semba's own slow body on those machines — the close dance whose name means party in Kimbundu is semba's child wearing zouk's electronics, not zouk slowed down. Cabo Verde folded its own sweetness in through coladeira's lilt and the morna-fed ache its romantic singers carry, and the modern wave took the genre to dance floors far beyond both countries. That history sets the register: tenderness first, sensuality suggested rather than said, saudade worn warm and upright. Kizomba misses, desires, and reconciles — it does not wail, and it never spells the heat out.

The rooms bend all of this. Clássica keeps semba's storytelling instinct and the answering guitar. Romântica / Moderna croons the melodic vow inside produced warmth. Tarraxinha strips the pulse to bass, breath, and murmur. The law above every dial is the same as everywhere: Portuguese is the song's language, but slang, Kimbundu or Creole costume, and accent spellings appear only if the user wrote them. Craft terms — the ghost note, the pickup, half-time, the groove cell — never enter lyrics or adlibs; the dance itself, though, is a legitimate subject whenever the user's story is about it, and render notes may name the instruments each room is built on. Delivery is directed as warmth and closeness in plain English — low, close, unhurried — never as an accent, and the user's own people, streets, and nights always outrank any postcard Africa.

## What disqualifies a Kizomba song

- No room to sway: every bar packed wall-to-wall with words, no air left for the guitar, the keyboard, or the beat to answer — kizomba is written around its silences and its slow pulse, and a sheet with no space is not kizomba.
- Wrong engine pasted on: a dembow boom-chick, trap hi-hat rolls, a log-drum amapiano bounce, a requinto-and-güira bachata engine, or carnival-fast zouk brightness under the words — the slow Angolan pulse with its ghosted pickup is the genre's floor, and replacing it re-routes the song out.
- Explicit heat: kizomba suggests — tenderness first, desire implied through closeness and one real detail; spelled-out sexuality disqualifies in every room and kills Tarraxinha outright, whose whole law is suggestion.
- Generated dialect or slang: Kimbundu, Cape Verdean Creole, or Angolan street slang the user never wrote — in the lyrics, adlibs, delivery directions, or render notes — is the cardinal sin; warmth and phrasing carry the identity.
- Postcard Africa: generic sunsets, drums, and brochure scenery standing in for the user's actual streets, people, and nights — vacation-poster kizomba is parody, whatever the beat does.
- Craft terms in the song: ghost note, pickup, half-time, or groove talk appearing in the lyrics or adlibs — the writer's vocabulary, never the song's; the dance itself, by contrast, is a beloved subject whenever the user's story is about it.
- Register breach: a raw undignified despair-wail or an angry public scene — kizomba keeps its dignity even heartbroken, and a collapse or a fight belongs to another genre's room, not this floor.
- Nobody in the arms: kizomba faces someone at close distance — a loved one, an absent one, one body in the room — and detached, stakes-free mood reporting has no home in any of the three rooms.
