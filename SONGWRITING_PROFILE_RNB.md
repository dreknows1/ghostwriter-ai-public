## How an R&B writer thinks

An R&B writer starts with who the song is spoken to. R&B is intimate by trade: the song is almost always aimed at one real person — a lover, an ex, a spouse, sometimes the narrator's own self — and every line should survive being said to that person's face. First person is the home position: one voice, one person, right now. Some rooms bend the tense or widen the address — to family, to spirit, to everybody listening — but somebody real is always on the receiving end.

Phrasing works the way people talk when they finally say the true thing: conversational sentences, not slogans. The genre's most important habit is leaving room — the voice is the lead instrument, and the words must get out of its way. Key lines like to end on open vowels the voice can hold and bend. Where the space goes is the room's call: some rooms stop lines early for runs, some leave long instrumental pockets, some leave one beat for an echoed word. A lyric that leaves no space anywhere — no line end, no bar, no section — has already failed as R&B.

Rhyme runs on a spectrum. In some rooms, slant rhymes — words that almost rhyme — and vowel echoes are the everyday tools, and a too-perfect rhyme reads as fake; in others, clean simple rhyme is the tradition and reads as timeless. The room sets that dial. The genre-wide law is smaller and firmer: the honest word outranks the rhyming word, every time.

This genre is the home of the double entendre — one line that means two things at once, both meanings intended, neither announced. When the user's story offers a true second meaning, use it; where it lands — the hook, an opening line, or an image carried across the song — is the room's and the story's call. A fully sincere song with no second meaning is just as much R&B. Flips — the expected phrase said the unexpected way — are welcome anywhere. Metaphors must be physically true and built from the user's own details, never decoration. Sensuality and vulnerability are the genre's twin subjects, and both work the same way: say the specific true thing plainly, and never announce that a moment is sexy or sad.

Verses talk and choruses lift — or, in some rooms, circle and deepen — and the lift is melodic and emotional, not automatically loud. Plan ad-lib room — answers, echoes, hums — and let the room say where it sits. Many rooms end on a vamp: a short section, usually the chorus, repeating while the lead sings freely over it. Whether a song vamps is the room's call; when it does, the chorus words must be simple and true enough to survive the repeats. The bridge is where the song changes — in many rooms the guard drops there, in others it is the turn in the story; the room decides.

Rendering, in any room, protects three things. Space: instruments leave air; a wall of sound kills intimacy. The voice up front: lead vocal close and human, background vocals treated as their own instrument — stacks, answers, echoes — with ad-libs written as sounds and delivery notes (hums, calls, breath), never slang. The pocket: a warm low end and a groove the phrasing was written to sit on. Every dial above bends to the user's story; none of it may ever change what the song is about.

## What disqualifies an R&B song

- No room for the voice: every bar is packed wall-to-wall with words, leaving no space anywhere for runs, ad-libs, or held notes — if the vocal cannot breathe, it is not R&B.
- Nobody on the other end: no specific person, the narrator's own self, or the narrator's own people and life is being addressed — detached, stakes-free reporting has no home in R&B.
- Melody does not lead: rapped or chanted bars carry the whole song with no sung through-line — that is hip-hop wearing R&B production.
- Zero vulnerability and zero desire: a song that never opens up and never wants anything is not R&B, whatever the drums do.
- The hook is a crowd slogan with no one on the receiving end — R&B choruses may be belted by many voices, but they are always sung to somebody.
- Slang or dialect the user never wrote, pasted on to sound R&B — identity must come from rhythm and phrasing in standard English; faked vocabulary marks the song as parody, not R&B.
