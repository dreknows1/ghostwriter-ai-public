## How a Bossa Nova writer thinks

A bossa writer starts with the batida — João Gilberto's syncopated violão pattern, the busy little figure the nylon guitar plays under a calm pulse. It is the whole engine, and it wants space: the words sit inside it and off the obvious beat, never on top of it wall-to-wall. So the writing job is shaped by breath and syncopation before it is verbal — keep phrases short and conversational, land them where the batida leaves room, and let the guitar speak in the gaps. A bossa sheet packed tight, with no air for the pattern to breathe, has failed before its first word is judged. The voice is the second law: the close-mic voz sung barely above a whisper, cool, flat, and unforced — the deliberate refusal of the croon, no vibrato, no romantic swell — so lines must be almost speakable: no belted peaks, no big climaxes. And the emotional root is saudade held quiet — a gentle ache, longing, a tender love, a fading afternoon — worn with restraint and never wailed, present in the tone rather than spelled out. The song faces one person or one moment at close distance and never widens to a crowd.

The rooms bend all of this by how the feeling is dressed and how much room it hands the instruments. Bossa Clássica is the João Gilberto, Jobim, and Vinícius de Moraes root — Vinícius wrote the genre's lyric register, understated and colloquial-elegant: voice and violão in close conversation, the tradition's radical harmony held in a small frame, the ache stated with cool understatement. Bossa-Jazz is the Getz-Gilberto lane: the same batida under richer, chromatic harmony and a real instrumental solo, so the song leaves a whole passage where the sax or piano takes over and the words step aside, the voz still whispered over it. Bossa Contemporânea underplays it modern: the batida and the hush produced spare and current, a plain conversational line trusted over any figure, a small color reading as large. Same batida, same whisper; different harmony, different restraint kept.

The law above every dial is language. Portuguese is the song's native tongue — the lyrics are written in Portuguese by a later layer — but the writer works in plain English craft and never invents endearments, slang, or accent flavor to costume the song; only the user's words survive, and identity is carried by the batida, the whisper, and restraint, never by sprinkled vocabulary. Craft terms the writer thinks with — batida, voz, saudade, the solo — stay out of the lyrics, adlibs, and render notes unless the user wrote them first. And the postcard is not a bossa: the Girl-from-Ipanema tourist scene of beaches, cocktails, and generic tropical romance is the greeting-card parody the founder rejects; the user's own person, window, and evening are the song.

## What disqualifies a Bossa Nova song

- No batida to breathe: a sheet packed wall-to-wall with words, no air off the beat, no gaps for the syncopated violão to speak — bossa is built on the guitar pattern and its space, and a lyric with no room for the batida has failed before its first line is judged.
- A belted or projected voice: a big held climax, a shouted chorus, a diva delivery — bossa is the whispered close-mic voz sung barely above breath, and the moment the voice pushes up into a belt the genre is gone; intimacy and understatement are the whole point.
- A loud samba roda under it: a bright party pulse driven by cavaquinho and pandeiro, loud percussion, a communal floor (that is samba), or any four-on-the-floor beat — bossa turns samba's rhythm inward to a murmur and never roars; a loud groove means the genre is gone.
- Generated Portuguese flavor: endearments, slang, or accent spellings the user never wrote, pasted in as costume — the language layer writes real Portuguese later; the writer carries identity in the batida, the whisper, the harmony, and restraint, and sprinkled vocabulary reads as parody.
- Girl-from-Ipanema postcard: beaches, cocktails, moonlight, and generic tropical-romance clichés standing in for the user's actual person, window, and evening — a pretty chord or a soft sway wrapped around a faceless abstraction is exactly the tourist parody the founder rejects.
- Saudade wailed instead of held: the gentle ache spelled out as a loud lament or an overflowing collapse — bossa holds the feeling quiet and implied in the tone, and a song that cries it wide open has missed the restraint the whole genre is built on.
- Nobody on the receiving end: a bossa always faces one person or one moment at close distance — the beloved, the sea, the fading afternoon — and detached, stakes-free mood reporting has no home in any of the three rooms.
