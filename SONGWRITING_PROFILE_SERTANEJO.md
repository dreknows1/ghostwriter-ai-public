## How a Sertanejo writer thinks

A sertanejo writer starts with a two-voice texture, not one voice. The genre's signature is the melody doubled in parallel thirds — a dupla singing the whole line together, or a solo star (Marília Mendonça, Gusttavo Lima, Luan Santana) whose records stack a segunda voz a third above on every hook. So the writing job is built for that doubling before it is verbal: keep the melody singable and stepwise, in a range that harmonizes sweetly a third apart, and hear the pair locking on the hook even when one name is on the record. A sertanejo line written as an unstacked lone belt, with no room for the second voice to track it, has missed the thing that makes the genre sound like itself. The other constant is the heart: dor de cotovelo, the ache of a love gone wrong, the root the music keeps returning to even when it celebrates.

The tradition spans a wide arc, and choosing the room is choosing where on that arc the song sits and how the ache is worn. Sertanejo Raiz holds the root: a dupla and a viola caipira on the porch, the moda-de-viola storytelling of the paulista interior, a rural story told slow with the ponteado answering between the lines and the ache present but narrated, not yet wailed. Sertanejo Universitário is the modern radio and arena lane: the same two voices plus a full band, swinging between festa and dor de cotovelo, a hook built for a whole crowd to sing back, the doubling in thirds now the arena's sound. Sofrência / Modão is the heavy heartbreak lane in two registers: the sofrência wailed wide open and undignified over a drink, and the classic modão-romântico — the Zezé Di Camargo & Luciano and Bruno & Marrone monument — pleading at full ache with its dignity intact, the sanfona crying back like a second mourner in both. Same two-voice texture, same heart; different world, different restraint.

The law above every dial is language. Portuguese is the song's native tongue — the lyrics are written in Portuguese by a later layer — but the writer works in plain English craft instruction and never invents a caipira drawl, slang, or accent flavor to costume the song; only words the user wrote survive, and identity is carried by the two-voice harmony, the accompaniment, and how nakedly the ache is worn, never by sprinkled vocabulary. Craft terms the writer thinks with — the dupla, thirds, the ponteado, the modão, dor de cotovelo as jargon — are working vocabulary and stay out of the lyrics and adlibs unless the user wrote them first; the render notes may and should name the instruments the room is built on (viola caipira, sanfona, violão). And the postcard is not a sertanejo: generic country-boy scenery, hillbilly clichés, and stock sad-song heartbreak are the parody the founder rejects; the user's own roça, road, bottle, and name are the song.

## What disqualifies a Sertanejo song

- No two-voice texture written for: a melody sitting in a range that cannot double sweetly in thirds, with no room for a segunda voz to track it on the hook — whether a dupla or a solo star with the stacked second voice, the doubling in thirds is the genre's signature, and a line that ignores it has missed the sound that makes sertanejo itself.
- Wrong tradition under it: a sanfona-and-zabumba São João dance with a triângulo (that is Forró), a cavaquinho-and-pandeiro samba party (that is Pagode), or any groove from a neighboring genre standing in — sertanejo is the Brazilian interior caipira tradition (interior São Paulo, Minas, and Goiás — Goiânia the modern capital) with the viola caipira or the modern band, and a neighbor's engine means the genre is gone.
- Generated caipira flavor: a fake country drawl, slang, or accent spellings the user never wrote, pasted in as costume — the language layer writes real Portuguese later; the writer carries identity in the two voices, the accompaniment, and the ache, and sprinkled dialect reads as parody.
- Postcard country costume: hillbilly scenery, generic country-boy clichés, and stock sad-song heartbreak standing in for the user's actual roça, road, bottle, and name — a viola or a big chorus wrapped around a faceless abstraction is exactly the tourist parody the founder rejects.
- Wrong scale and restraint for the room: a spare porch moda blown up into an arena hook, a bar-counter collapse forced to keep radio composure, or a crowd-sized festa shrunk to a lone viola — the rooms differ by accompaniment and how openly the ache is worn, and missing that flattens the song.
- Nobody on the receiving end, or an ache that is not the user's own: a sertanejo faces someone — a room being told a story, an arena crowd, or the one who left — and detached, stakes-free mood reporting, or a stock heartbreak swapped in for the user's real wound, has no home in any of the three rooms.
