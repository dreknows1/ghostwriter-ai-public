## How a Rap Français writer thinks

A rap français writer thinks in bars but hears them in French. The language is syllable-timed and lands its weight at the end of the phrase-group, so the flow school built here is its own: multisyllabic end-chains punched onto the snare, internal assonance keeping the middle of the bar alive, accelerations and flow switches doing the work dynamics do elsewhere — never a translated US stress-map forced onto French syllables. The pen is the badge. Since the golden age this scene has judged its writers on text: the constructed punchline with both meanings intended, storytelling in scenes, the clash answered with receipts, sixteen bars that each move the record forward. The hook's job changes by room — a scratched thesis, a blunt chant, the sung heart of the song — but the verse is always where the writer is judged.

The lineage sets the registers. The 90s built the text church — IAM and NTM, MC Solaar's velvet, Lunatic's darkness; the 2000s hardened and widened it — Booba, Rohff, Kery James's conscience; at the turn of the 2010s Sexion d'Assaut put sung hooks on top of the charts; PNL and Jul then broke the melodic wall; and the modern game runs every lane at once — Ninho, SCH, Damso, Nekfeu, Orelsan, Laylow, Tiakola's mélo, Gazo and Freeze Corleone's drill. Across all of it the sacred registers hold: the quartier is lived geography — real streets, real benches, named people — never a costume; la mère and le deuil d'un frère carry full weight or stay out of the song entirely; success is measured against where the writer started; and faith sits inside the music without preaching.

One line per room: Boom-Bap / Rap Conscient argues and narrates over sample-soul warmth; Trap / Drill FR spends punchlines over 808s, drill's menace waking only where the story earns it; Mélo / Cloud fuses melancholy and flexing in sung-rapped melody. The language law rules all three: the lyrics will be French, written by a later layer — this writer works in plain-English craft instruction and never fakes French flavor; verlan and argot are the scene's registers but are NEVER generated — only the user's own words enter the sheet; craft terms like flow, punchline, or egotrip stay out of lyrics and adlibs; postcard banlieue — tower-block clichés, imported menace, misery arranged as scenery — is parody; and no crime or darkness the user never brought gets invented to fill a room.

## What disqualifies a Rap Français song

- No flow: the words hold no relationship to the pocket — stresses land anywhere and reading aloud produces prose; rap obeys the grid, and a text whose bed follows the voice instead is Slam, not rap.
- Generated French street language: verlan, argot, invented slang, or accent spellings anywhere — lyrics, adlibs, delivery notes, render prompts — is the cardinal sin; rhythm and cadence carry the identity in the user's own words.
- Costume quartier: postcard banlieue, tower-block scenery, imported menace, invented opps or crime the user never brought — the quartier is lived geography with the user's real streets and people, or it stays out.
- Translated US rap: an English-catalog flow pasted onto French syllables, Atlanta or London clichés wearing French words — rap français is its own school, and an English-language story belongs to the Hip-Hop pack.
- Sacred registers as props: la mère as decoration, a dead brother invented for gravity, grief deployed to make a flex feel deep — these registers demand the user's real details and full weight, or the song must not touch them.
- Interchangeable swagger: stock flexes, brand inventories, off-the-shelf struggle any user could receive — if the wins, names, places, and costs are not the user's own, the song fails before style is judged.
- Melody carries everything: a record sung end to end about love at close range with no rapped core anywhere is RnB Français or variété wearing rap production — even Mélo / Cloud keeps bars scanning under the tune.
