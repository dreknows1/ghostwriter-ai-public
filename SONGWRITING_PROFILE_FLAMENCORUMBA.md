## How a Flamenco / Rumba writer thinks

A flamenco writer starts with the compás — the rhythmic cycle, often twelve beats, counted not by a click but by clapping palmas, and everything answers to it. The cante rides that cycle over the flamenco guitar, and the writing job is rhythmic and spatial before it is verbal: scan each line against the compás, land the stressed syllables where the cycle wants them, and end phrases early so the guitar can cry its reply in the gap. A sheet packed wall-to-wall, with no air for the guitar to answer and no room for the voice to hold and break, has failed before its first word is judged. The palmas are the pulse the whole song sits inside; lose them and the flamenco is gone.

The tradition the genre was built to carry is the quejío — the cry, the wound of grief, exile, or a ruined love worn open in the voice. That is the emotional root, but the three rooms wear it three different ways, and choosing the room is choosing how nakedly the cry shows and to whom. Puro cries it wide open — the deep song, cante jondo, on a slow elastic cycle where a line hangs on a held note and the guitar answers like a second mourner, no dignity kept and no crowd to please. Rumba turns the cycle festive and four-square and hands the feeling to a clapping circle — the Gipsy Kings party lane, warmth and joy sung outward, the guitars and palmas driving a fiesta. Fusión keeps a real compás under a modern beat and styles the cry into a contemporary hook — the Rosalía-adjacent lane, composure and attitude over a flamenco root, the melisma carried into a pop shape. Not one cycle but a family of compases carrying the same cante; different amount of grief shown, different distance to the listener.

The law above every dial is language and voice. The lyrics are written in Spanish later; the writer works in plain English and never invents accent spellings, gitano slang, or Andalusian flavor to costume the song — only the user's own words survive, and the identity is carried by the compás, the phrasing, and the guitar's answer, never by sprinkled vocabulary. Faked phonetics are the cardinal sin. The jaleo — the encouraging shouts a room throws to lift the cante — is legitimate energy, but directed as emotion and support in plain English, never as phonetic costume. Craft words the writer thinks with — compás, palo, quejío, jaleo, soleá, bulería, cante jondo, palmas — stay out of the lyrics, adlibs, and render notes unless the user wrote them first. And the postcard is not flamenco: castanets, roses, swirling dresses, and generic Spanish-guitar romance are the tourist parody the founder rejects; the user's own grief, fiesta, name, and place are the song.

## What disqualifies a Flamenco / Rumba song

- No compás, no palmas: a song with no rhythmic cycle and no clap under it, or a sheet packed so tight the guitar has no gap to cry back into — the compás counted in palmas and the guitar answering the voice are the genre's spine, and a lyric with no cycle and no air has failed before its first line is judged.
- Faked Andalusian phonetics or accent: gitano slang, accent spellings, or invented dialect flavor the user never wrote, pasted in as costume — the language layer writes real Spanish later; the writer carries identity in the compás and the phrasing, and faked phonetics are the cardinal sin.
- Castanets-and-roses postcard: swirling dresses, roses, castanet clichés, and generic Spanish-guitar romance standing in for the user's actual grief, fiesta, name, and place — tourist flamenco is fake whatever the guitar does.
- Wrong spine entirely: swapping the flamenco guitar and palmas for a clave and montuno (that is salsa), a dembow (that is reggaeton), or a pretty metered Spanish-guitar ballad with no cycle and no clap — flamenco is the raw cante over the flamenco guitar on a real compás, and losing that loses the genre.
- The quejío faked or flattened: grief worn as a generic sad mood with no real wound under it, or a deep-song cry kept at a dignified distance when Puro's whole register is the soul crying open — the cry has to be the user's actual pain, not a costume of sadness.
- Nobody on the receiving end: flamenco always faces someone — the absent one mourned, the fiesta clapping in a circle, or the modern audience — and detached, stakes-free mood reporting has no home in any of the three rooms.
