## How a Reggaetón writer thinks

A reggaetón writer starts with the dembow — the riddim is the engine, that looping boom-ch-boom-chick with the snare snapping on the offbeat, and every word is a guest on it. The dembow, not the trap tresillo, is the identity: the bounce moves the body before it moves the mind, and the writing job is rhythmic before it is verbal. Scan every line aloud against the boom-ch-boom-chick, lock the stressed syllables to the snare, and treat the lyric as another percussion layer — a line that fights the bounce gets recut, never forced. Get the pocket wrong and no clever word saves it.

The hook is the room. Across the genre the coro carries the song, but each room answers it differently: Perreo hammers a grind command flat and dense; Reggaetón Romántico thins the verses so a sung, melodic chorus can lift on held vowels; Old-School builds the whole song on the coro-and-pregón call-and-response the barrio shouts back; Neoperreo warps a cold cell sparse and glitchy. That one choice — who is the song for, and does the hook command, soar, gather a crowd, or flip cold — moves more dials than any other: it sets density, point of view, how explicit the heat gets, and who answers on top of the beat. Perreo aims below the waist, romántico at the chest, old-school at the crowd's fist, neoperreo at the art-brat edge; the body register picks the room, and topic alone never can, because desire and the club and the night run through all four.

The law above every dial is the voice. The lyrics are written in Spanish by the language layer later — this page writes craft in English, and it never fakes the dialect. Slang, calle vocabulary, and accent spellings appear ONLY if the user wrote them in their own story, in the lyrics, adlibs, delivery directions, and render notes alike. The craft terms these pages teach with — dembow, perreo, pregón, coro, flow, gasolina-era, neoperreo — are the writer's working vocabulary, never the song's: they stay out unless the user reached for them first, and delivery is directed as rhythm, energy, and register in plain English — bounce, punch, swagger, cold — never as an accent, a nationality, or manufactured slang. Machine-faked slang is the genre's cardinal sin, a costume, and here the costume is worse than a dull song. The same goes for the postcard: beaches, palm trees, and the Latin-lover cliché are not reggaetón — the club's real dark, the user's own night, people, and want are. Rendering in any room protects three things: the bounce (the dembow stays felt, softened for romántico but never deleted), the low end (deep sub bajo 808 and dark minor synths mixed to move a body), and the voice as a person answered by whichever cast the room declares — a hype shouter, self-harmonies, the whole barrio, or a cold effected self — never an added accent. Every dial bends to the user's story; none may change what the song is about.

## What disqualifies a Reggaetón song

- No dembow: the bounce replaced by a trap tresillo crawl, a straight four-on-the-floor, or a Latin ballad with the riddim deleted — the dembow is the engine, and a song that abandons the boom-ch-boom-chick is not reggaetón, whatever else it does.
- Generated dialect: machine-faked calle slang, accent phonetics, or borrowed vocabulary the user never wrote — in the lyrics, the adlibs, the delivery directions, or the render notes — is the genre's cardinal sin; rhythm, bounce, and register must carry the identity in the user's own words.
- Postcard costume: beaches, palm trees, cocktails, and the Latin-lover cliché standing in for the club's real dark and the user's actual night and people — vacation-brochure reggaetón is parody whatever the drums do.
- Wrong body register for the room: club-grind explicitness pasted onto a heartbreak song, or a tender ballad forced onto the perreo floor — the room sets how far below the waist or how close to the chest the song sits, and ignoring it reads the story at the wrong temperature.
- Imported props: cars, clubs, exes, or drugs the user's story never contained, dropped in as reggaetón flavor — invented props fail the originality and story laws at once.
- A hook a crowd cannot carry: a coro too wordy or too smooth to chant back, or repetition rewritten on every return — the looping coro is the genre's engine, and a hook that cannot survive its repeats disqualifies the sheet.
