## How a Pop Brasileiro writer thinks

A pop brasileiro writer starts from the refrão, because the chorus is the event — the earworm a whole country sings by the second pass. Streaming sharpened this into law: the intro is short, verse one gets to the point, and the chorus or its melodic core lands inside the first minute, because a skip is a verdict. The title sits at the top of the refrão or on its held peak, the post-chorus carries one small vocal idea planned at the writing stage, and a song whose chorus is not the high point of the writing has missed the genre before a word is judged. Portuguese does half the work when the writer lets it: the language is built of open vowels and the singable nasal ão, so peaks land on sounds a voice can hold and run through — and the same gift hides the genre's two oldest traps, the chained infinitive rhymes and the stock ão pairs every lazy sheet reaches for. The cure is always the same: rhyme on the concrete nouns of the user's own story.

The second thing the writer trusts is the levada. Brazilian pop swings even at its squarest — the pocket leans and the singer sits a breath behind the beat, unhurried and confident — so lines are written to ride the groove with gaps that let it breathe, never to sit on a grid. The register is direct: first person, plainspoken, unafraid. Brazilian pop cries in public and winks in the same night — the open ache it inherited from sofrência and the playful sass of deboche are both home registers, delivered as energy and attitude in plain English, never as slang the user did not write. The genre lives surrounded by stronger gravities and borrows from all of them — a funk-tinged swing, a sertanejo tear, a samba sway — but never surrenders: the moment the tamborzão leads, the song is funk carioca; the moment the poem outranks the hook, it is MPB. The refrão stays in charge.

The rooms bend all of this. Pop de Rádio runs bright and lands the feeling on voices singing together. Balada Pop drops to piano and climbs to one held note, wearing the hurt openly and planning the recovery. Pop Dançante hands the song to the body, a circling loop answered hotter each pass. The laws above every dial: lyrics are written in Portuguese by the language layer later; craft terms — refrão, levada, hook, drop, deboche — are the writer's tools and never enter lyrics or adlibs, though render notes may name instruments; postcard-Brazil is the tourist parody the founder rejects; and the user's story outranks every dial — one real detail from their life anchors even the biggest radio hook.

## What disqualifies a Pop Brasileiro song

- No chorus event: verses that never lift into a refrão, or a hook too wordy or too clever to sing back by the second pass — the singalong chorus is the genre's engine, and a song where the chorus is not the high point of the writing has failed whatever else it does.
- Generated dialect or slang: Brazilian gíria, accent spellings, or street costume the user never wrote — in the lyrics, the adlibs, the delivery directions, or the render notes — is the cardinal sin; melody, the levada, and the user's own words carry the flavor.
- Postcard-Brazil costume: beach, samba, and carnival clichés standing in for the user's actual street, night, and people — tourist-brochure Brazil is parody whatever the production does.
- Lane surrender: the naked tamborzão leading or an MC baile chant replacing the sung hook (that is funk carioca), a poem outranking the hook with no chorus engineering (that is MPB), a drunk bar-counter collapse with a dupla trading verses (that is sertanejo sofrência) — pop brasileiro borrows flavor from its neighbors and keeps the refrão in charge.
- Wrong temperature: a devastation sanded into a sunny bop, a night of freedom slowed into a ballad, a private ache ordered to dance — the room decides how the feeling arrives, and ignoring it reads the user's story wrong.
- The lazy sheet: chained infinitive rhymes and stock ão pairs carrying floating feeling-words with nothing real underneath — greeting-card pop is the genre's oldest parody, and one concrete detail from the user's story is the cure; imported props the story never contained fail the originality and story laws at once.
- Craft terms in the song: hook, drop, post-chorus, levada, or deboche appearing in the lyrics or adlibs — these are the writer's vocabulary, never the song's, and a sheet that sings them has confused the tool for the music.
