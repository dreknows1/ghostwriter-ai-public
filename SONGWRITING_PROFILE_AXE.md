## How an Axé writer thinks

An axé writer starts with a crowd that is already moving. The word itself is a blessing — life force, from the candomblé greeting — and the song is a machine for passing it: one voice on a truck or in front of a drum bloco, a street that answers. So the writing job is physical before it is verbal: for every line the writer asks what a hundred thousand bodies do on it — jump, answer, sway, hold a vowel — and a line with no physical answer gets recut. Hooks are commands and responses, line-ends open into big vowels a crowd can land on together, and the sheet is planned around its silences, because the timbau fills, the brass stabs, the guitarra baiana riffs, and above all the crowd's own mouth need the gaps.

The band is a drum city with an electric edge: timbau cracking on top, repique calling the turns, surdos underneath, agogô and percussão afro riding over them, plus the guitarra baiana — the little solid-body lead born when Dodô and Osmar drove amplified frevo through Salvador's 1950 Carnaval on the fobica, an old Ford, the seed of the trio elétrico — with brass and keys around it. Two pulses run the genre, and the writer picks one first: the trio's driving sprint or samba-reggae's heavier, Afro-rooted walking swing, because phrasing, hook speed, and word count are recut for each. Arrangement is a writing tool — the ramp into the chorus and the paradinha, the planned drop where the band cuts and the crowd sings alone, are written into the lyric, not added later.

The rooms bend all of this. Axé clássico writes the trio anthem: short fused verses, a command hook, the multidão as second voice. Samba-reggae writes pride and roots at walking pace: verses that narrate like chapters, a chorus a street can hold, the surdo choir as the second melody, joy as resistance. Axé pop / verão writes the summer hit: conversational verses, an engineered ramp, a chorus that works at a beach kiosk and on a trio at once.

The law above every dial is the same as everywhere. Portuguese is the song's language, written by a later layer; the writer works in plain English craft and never fakes Bahian slang, exclamations, or accent flavor — energy is directed as joy in plain English, and only the user's own words survive into the song. Craft terms as arrangement jargon — paradinha, resposta — are the writer's tools and never enter lyrics or adlibs; but bloco and trio (elétrico) are the genre's own beloved scene vocabulary, fully welcome in the song whenever the user's story lives on the avenue — half the canon names the trio and the bloco by name. And joy is the engine, never the subject: the user's story, whatever it is, is converted into lift, never replaced by props — postcard-Bahia, the beach-and-folklore souvenir, is the tourist parody this genre rejects on sight.

## What disqualifies an Axé song

- No room for the answer: a sheet packed wall-to-wall, with no gaps for the crowd's response, the timbau fills, the brass stabs, or the guitarra baiana riff — axé is written around the crowd's mouth and the drums' answers, and a lyric with no air has failed before its first word is judged.
- A hook that cannot be obeyed or lifted: too wordy, too clever, or too slow to chant — the axé chorus is a physical event for a crowd already moving, and a hook that only works seated is not axé.
- Lost pulse: neither the trio's drive nor samba-reggae's walking swing under the words — a straight club grid, a tamborzão, a dembow, or a Rio roda cast means the song belongs to another lane, whatever the lyric says.
- Generated Bahian flavor: slang, exclamations, or accent spellings the user never wrote — in the lyrics, adlibs, delivery directions, or render notes — is the cardinal sin; energy is delivered as joy in plain English, and sprinkled costume reads as parody.
- Postcard-Bahia: beaches, folklore props, and tourist-brochure scenery standing in for the user's actual story and people — touristy writing is rejected by name here; the real Salvador in an axé song is the one the user brought.
- Craft terms as jargon in the song: paradinha or resposta appearing in the lyrics or adlibs — arrangement tools never enter the song (render notes may name instruments); but bloco and trio elétrico are legitimate scene words whenever the story is on the avenue, never banned.
- Joy missing at the engine: a song that faces no one — irony, detachment, or a lament that sinks and stays down — axé converts every story into lift for a crowd that answers, and a sheet with nobody to answer it has no home in any of the three rooms.
