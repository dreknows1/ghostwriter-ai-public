## How an Afrobeats writer thinks

An Afrobeats writer starts with the groove, not the sentence. The percussion is already talking before the first word arrives — the swung kick, the shakers, the space between them — and the writer's job is to find where the words dance on that pattern. Every line gets read aloud against the drums: stressed syllables land with the percussion hits, and the voice sits slightly behind the beat, relaxed, never chasing. A line that fights the groove is not fixed by singing it harder; it is recut until it dances. The melody and the drums are co-writers here, and words are chosen as much for how the mouth rides them as for what they say.

Economy is the second instinct. Phrases run short — a few strong words that survive being sung four times in a row — and they end early, leaving the last beats of the bar to the shakers, the answers, and the air. The gap is written property: the room decides who lives there (a communal chant answer, the singer's own hazy layer, or the log drum itself), but the writer cuts the hole on purpose. Open vowels and soft consonants carry the sweetness; a plain line that glides beats a clever line that crowds the mouth.

Repetition is architecture, not a failure — this genre holds the curriculum's sanctioned bend. The hook can be half the song; verse two may deepen the same feeling instead of advancing a plot; freshness comes from how the melody bends on each return, how the voices stack, and what gets subtracted underneath. But the bend has a floor: the verses still carry the user's real details — the actual name, the actual blessing, the actual city — because repetition deepens a real story; it never replaces one. A loop with nothing true under it is a jingle.

The home registers are celebration, desire, and hustle-blessings, and they mix freely: gratitude sits inside a love song, blessings inside a success song, and thankfulness never clashes with wanting. The address stays simple and direct — a lover, God, life itself, or the room — and the chosen room sets how communal or interior it gets: one lane keeps the feeling sweet and universal with a crowd invited to answer, one turns inward to the awkward private detail, one compresses everything to the feeling of a single night on the floor.

Performance is planned on the page, and every room fields a different cast: mainline writes gaps for communal chant answers, alté layers the singer's own voice into a haze, amapiano leaves space so the log drum can answer instead of a voice. Ad-libs obey the dialect law like everything else: sounds, hums, chants, and short honest answers in the song's own plain voice.

Identity is carried by rhythm, phrasing, and melodic shape in standard English — a law, not a preference. Pidgin appears only if the user wrote it in their own story; generated dialect, borrowed exclamations, and postcard-Africa scenery read as costume, and in this genre a costume is worse than a dull song. Rendering protects the same values: percussion mixed up where it can talk, a warm rounded low end, air between elements, and the voice silky and unhurried on top.

## What disqualifies an Afrobeats song

- No space for the percussion: every bar packed wall-to-wall with words, no gap left at line ends for the shakers, the answers, or the groove — if the percussion never gets the last beats of a bar, it is not Afrobeats.
- Words with no relationship to the groove: stressed syllables landing anywhere, lines that cannot be sung relaxed and slightly behind the beat — read aloud against the drums, the lyric must already dance.
- Repetition with nothing under it: the genre's sanctioned bend deepens a real story — a hook looping over verses that carry no real details from the user's life is a jingle wearing the genre, and it fails the originality law too.
- Nothing to photograph: a song built from floating sweetness — love, joy, blessings, good vibes — with no real place, person, object, or physical action from the user's life anywhere in it; the concrete-image law applies at full force in a feel-good genre, because pretty nothing over a groove is still a greeting card.
- A plot that needs a timeline: events in order, scenes, a second act — every room's word budget is too small; this genre circles one feeling, and a story that needs narrative room has outgrown it.
- No sung melodic through-line: fully rapped or talked bars carrying the whole song is hip-hop or dancehall wearing afro percussion — even the sparsest chant room keeps its cells melodic.
- Costume identity: pidgin, slang, or African-language exclamations the user never wrote — in lyrics, adlibs, or render vocabulary — or safari, jungle, and postcard-Africa imagery; rhythm and phrasing carry the identity in standard English, and faked vocabulary marks the song as parody, not Afrobeats.
