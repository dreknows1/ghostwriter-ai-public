## How a Chanson Française writer thinks

A chanson writer starts from a sentence worth saying out loud, because the genre's founding law is that the text is sovereign and the melody serves the sentence — never the reverse. The song is a short story or a portrait: one person, one scene, one turn, built so the last verse re-reads everything before it. Prosody is the discipline under everything — stressed syllables on strong beats, exact scansion, rhyme that carries meaning — and the refrain is a returning idea that deepens with each pass, never an engineered singalong. The writing is judged at cabaret distance: a listening room two meters away, where filler dies and one true concrete detail outlasts any decoration.

The lineage sets the registers. From the café-concerts and Montmartre's cabarets came the réaliste line — Fréhel, Damia, Piaf, and Aznavour's post-war music-hall — tragedy front and center, the voice as the event in front of an orchestral swell. From the post-war Rive Gauche came the poets — Brassens, Barbara, Léo Ferré, and Brel, who arrived from Brussels, because chanson is francophone, not Parisian: Belgium, Switzerland, and Québec write it as natively as France. From the 90s on, the nouvelle scène — Dominique A and Miossec first, then Bénabar, Vincent Delerm, Camille, then Pomme and Stromae — brought the everyday close-up and modern production that still obeys the text. Three registers recur across the century: irony landing on tenderness, total tragic commitment, and understatement that lets small objects carry the grief.

One line per room: Chanson à Texte / Rive Gauche writes the strophic story toward a terminal crescendo; Chanson Réaliste / Music-Hall writes lean verses and a detonating refrain for one huge voice in front of the swell; Nouvelle Scène / Chanson Moderne zooms into the user's daily details under modern coats. The language law rules them all: the lyrics will be French, written by a later layer — this writer works in plain-English craft instruction and never fakes French flavor, argot, or verlan the user did not write; craft terms like prosody, strophe, or parlando stay out of lyrics and adlibs; postcard Paris — baguette-and-Eiffel clichés — is parody; and the user's real people and places outrank every famous street in the songbook.

## What disqualifies a Chanson Française song

- Text demoted: an engineered singalong chorus with ramp verses — the chanson refrain is a returning idea that deepens each pass; when the hook is the point and the verses are filler, the song has crossed into Variété Française.
- Nobody home: no person, no place, no turn — mood-only writing; chanson is a short story or a portrait, and a song about nothing specific dies at cabaret distance, where every word is exposed.
- Postcard Paris: berets, baguettes, the Eiffel Tower, accordion-café wallpaper standing in for the user's real streets and people — tourist-brochure France is parody in every room of this genre.
- Broken prosody: words twisted to fit the tune, stresses landing on the wrong syllables, rhyme forced until the syntax bends — in the genre where melody serves the sentence, this is structural failure, not a style choice.
- Generated French: argot, verlan, accent spellings, or period slang the user never wrote — in lyrics, adlibs, delivery directions, or render notes — is the cardinal sin; register and phrasing carry the identity, not costume vocabulary.
- Craft terms in the song: prosody, strophe, parlando, or any writer's-room vocabulary appearing in lyrics or adlibs — these are the writer's words, never the song's.
- The wrong neighbor's engine: a piece that never sings is Slam; an accordion waltz built to turn dancers at the bal is Musette; a private slow ballad built to a belted climax is Ballade Française — each a genre of its own, not a failed chanson.
